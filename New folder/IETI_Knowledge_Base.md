# SECTION 1: MOLD INSPECTION & TESTING — COMPLETE TECHNICAL REFERENCE

## 1.1 The Mold Inspection Process (Step by Step)

### Step 1: Pre-Inspection Interview (15–20 minutes)
Before the inspector arrives on site, a structured intake interview is conducted. The inspector or scheduling coordinator asks about: symptom timeline (when occupants started feeling ill, whether symptoms improve when away from the building), any known water intrusion events (roof leaks, plumbing failures, flooding), previous mold remediation history, building age and construction type, and HVAC system type. This information shapes the sampling strategy before the inspector walks through the door.

### Step 2: Visual Assessment (30–90 minutes depending on building size)
The inspector performs a systematic room-by-room visual survey. The inspector looks for: visible mold growth (discoloration, fuzzy or powdery growth on surfaces), water staining (yellow/brown stains on ceilings, walls, around windows), efflorescence on concrete or masonry (white mineral deposits indicating water migration), deteriorating or bubbling paint (indicating moisture behind surfaces), musty odors (indicator of active or historical mold growth even without visible evidence), and condensation patterns. The inspector documents everything photographically. A standard residential inspection at this stage covers all rooms, attic, crawlspace, basement, and mechanical rooms.

### Step 3: Moisture Mapping (20–45 minutes)
This is one of the most technically important steps. Equipment used:

- **Protimeter MMS2 or Tramex MEP moisture meter (non-invasive pin-type and scan-type):** Reads relative moisture units (RMU) in building materials. Readings above 16–17% wood moisture content indicate elevated moisture. Drywall readings are measured differently and a "wet" indication on scan mode prompts further investigation.
- **Flir E8 or E6 infrared thermal imaging camera:** Detects evaporative cooling on wet surfaces and thermal anomalies that indicate hidden moisture behind walls or ceilings. Thermal imaging does NOT directly detect mold — it detects temperature differentials that suggest moisture. A cold spot on drywall after a rain event, for example, strongly suggests active water intrusion.
- **Sling psychrometer or digital hygrometer (Extech RH200 or similar):** Measures ambient relative humidity and temperature. Sustained indoor RH above 60% supports mold growth. The inspector records temperature and RH in each area sampled to contextualize air sample results.

### Step 4: Sampling Strategy Development
Based on the visual and moisture findings, the inspector determines which sampling methods to use, how many samples, and where. Sampling strategy is not arbitrary — it follows a logic: areas of suspected mold get surface samples or wall cavity samples; all inspections include at least one outdoor air sample for baseline comparison; areas of occupant complaint get air samples; post-remediation projects follow a specific clearance protocol.

### Step 5: Sample Collection (30–60 minutes)
Specific sampling methods are described in detail in Section 1.2. During collection, the inspector maintains chain-of-custody documentation, records all field data on the lab submission form (volume sampled, cassette lot numbers, field blanks if applicable), and stores samples appropriately (refrigerate biological samples, room temperature for bulk/tape lift).

### Step 6: Lab Submission
Samples are shipped via overnight courier to the accredited laboratory. IETI uses AIHA-accredited laboratories (EMLab P&K, Eurofins, Environix). Chain-of-custody forms accompany all samples. Rush turnaround (24–48 hours) is available at additional cost. Standard turnaround is 3–5 business days.

### Step 7: Report Writing and Quality Review (1–2 days post-results)
The inspector compiles a comprehensive report incorporating field observations, photos, moisture readings, lab results, and professional interpretation. The report is not simply a printout of lab data — it includes the inspector's professional opinion on significance, probable sources, and recommendations based on IICRC S520 standards.

### Step 8: Report Delivery and Consultation
Reports are delivered digitally within 7–8 business days of the inspection (or sooner with rush processing). A follow-up phone or video consultation is included to walk the client through findings, answer questions, and clarify remediation priorities.

## 1.2 Sampling Methods — Detailed Comparison

### Air Sampling: Spore Trap Cassettes
The most common air sampling method for mold assessment. A calibrated air pump pulls a known volume of air through a spore trap cassette, impacting airborne particles onto a collection surface (typically a glass slide coated with adhesive).

**Cassette Types:**
- **Air-O-Cell (Zefon International):** The industry standard for indoor mold air sampling. Single-use cassette, accepts 75 liters of air at 15 liters/minute for a 5-minute sample. Analysis by direct microscopy — a trained analyst reads the glass slide and identifies and counts spore types. Results reported in spores per cubic meter (spores/m³).
- **Burkard cassette:** Alternative brand, similar principle. Used interchangeably with Air-O-Cell in most protocols.
- **RCS+ (Biotest):** Centrifugal impaction sampler using a rotating impeller. Less common in field inspections but used in some research and pharmaceutical environments. More sensitive to viable organisms.

**Viable vs. Non-Viable Sampling:**
- **Non-viable (spore trap):** Counts all spores — dead and alive. Fast, inexpensive, most commonly used. Does not distinguish between species within genera (e.g., all Penicillium/Aspergillus spores look similar under direct microscopy — reported as a combined group "Pen/Asp").
- **Viable (culture-based):** Air is impacted onto growth media (typically MEA — malt extract agar, or DG18 for xerophilic species). Viable spores germinate and are identified by morphology after 5–10 days incubation. More specific identification, can identify exact species (critical for distinguishing Aspergillus fumigatus from benign species). Takes longer and adds cost. IETI uses viable sampling when species-level identification is clinically important (e.g., immunocompromised client, suspected Stachybotrys).

**Standard Sample Volume:** 75 liters (15 L/min × 5 minutes). In areas suspected of very high contamination, a 25-liter sample may be taken to avoid overloading the cassette. In very clean environments, up to 150 liters may be sampled to increase sensitivity.

**Outdoor Baseline:** Every mold inspection requires at minimum one outdoor air sample collected upwind from the building. This establishes the ambient background level of outdoor mold spores for comparison. Without an outdoor baseline, indoor results cannot be properly interpreted.

### Surface Sampling

**Tape Lift:**
A length of clear adhesive tape is pressed firmly against the suspect surface and transferred to a glass microscope slide. Used for: visible mold growth identification, quality assurance of remediation (did the surface get cleaned?), and substrate characterization. Analyzed by direct microscopy. Brands include Pro-Lab and Zefon tape lift kits. Results identify spore types and/or fungal structures present. Tape lift is preferred over swab for smooth, accessible surfaces with visible growth.

**Swab Sampling:**
A sterile swab moistened with sterile water or buffer solution is wiped over a defined area (typically 10 cm × 10 cm template). Used when: the surface is rough or irregular (rough concrete, textured surfaces) where tape lift cannot make good contact; when the question is presence/absence rather than visual identification; when the lab will culture the sample. Less preferred than tape lift for smooth surfaces because recovery efficiency is lower.

**Bulk Sampling:**
A physical piece of the suspect material (chunk of drywall, piece of flooring material, ceiling tile, wood sample) is collected and submitted for laboratory analysis. The lab homogenizes the material and examines it for fungal structures and sometimes cultures it. Used when: the question is whether building materials have become contaminated through their full depth; during pre-demolition assessment; for legal or insurance documentation.

### ERMI (Environmental Relative Moldiness Index)

ERMI was developed by the EPA's Dr. Stephen Vesper and colleagues as a dust-based, DNA-analysis screening tool. It is NOT a replacement for air sampling — it is a complementary tool particularly useful for occupant exposure assessment.

**Collection:** Dust is collected from a 1-square-meter area of floor using the ERMI Swiffer-based collection method (a commercially available Swiffer-type cloth sweeps the defined area), or alternatively by vacuuming through a special collection cassette. The sample is sent to an ERMI-certified laboratory (EMLab P&K offers ERMI analysis).

**Analysis:** PCR (polymerase chain reaction) analysis quantifies the DNA of 36 specific mold species in the dust. Results are reported as spores-equivalent per gram of dust.

**The 36-Species Panel:**
- **Group 1 (26 species — Water Damage Indicator Species):** These are molds associated with water damage and moisture intrusion. Key Group 1 species include: Stachybotrys chartarum, Chaetomium globosum, Aspergillus niger, Aspergillus ochraceus, Aspergillus versicolor, Penicillium chrysogenum/rubens, Penicillium variabile, Trichoderma viride, Cladosporium sphaerospermum, Eurotium amstelodami, Scopulariopsis brevicaulis, and others. High Group 1 scores drive a high ERMI score.
- **Group 2 (10 species — Common Outdoor/Background Species):** These are ubiquitous outdoor species. Key Group 2 species: Cladosporium cladosporioides (types 1 and 2), Alternaria alternata, Epicoccum nigrum, Chrysonilia sitophila. Group 2 spores are subtracted in the ERMI calculation.

**ERMI Score Calculation:** ERMI = Log(Sum of Group 1 species) − Log(Sum of Group 2 species). Scores range approximately from −10 to +20. Interpretation: Below 0 = relatively clean; 0–5 = moderate, warrants attention; Above 5 = elevated, consistent with water-damaged building. Above 8–10 = significantly elevated.

**HERTSMI-2:** A 5-species subset of ERMI developed by Dr. Ritchie Shoemaker for use in CIRS (Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome) assessment. The 5 species are: Stachybotrys chartarum, Aspergillus penicillioides, Aspergillus versicolor, Chaetomium globosum, and Wallemia sebi. HERTSMI-2 score below 11 is considered safe for most CIRS patients; 11–15 is caution; above 16 is contraindicated for re-entry for highly sensitive individuals.

### Wall Cavity Sampling (Without Demolition)
When moisture mapping indicates elevated moisture within wall cavities and visual access is not available, IETI can collect air samples from within the wall cavity. Method: a 3/16-inch hole is drilled into the drywall at the base of the wall (below any insulation if possible), a flexible extension tube is attached to the Air-O-Cell cassette and inserted into the cavity, and a standard 75-liter sample is collected. This is highly effective at detecting hidden mold growth within wall cavities and avoids the cost and disruption of opening walls for a screening-level assessment.

### Settled Plate Sampling — Why IETI Does NOT Use This Method
Settled plate (or "gravity plate") sampling involves leaving an open petri dish of culture media in a room for a set period (typically 1 hour) and then incubating it to see what grows. IETI does not use this method for the following technical reasons: (1) Collection is entirely passive and dependent on air movement — mold spores settle by gravity and random air currents, not by systematic sampling. (2) There is no way to calculate spores per cubic meter because no defined air volume is sampled — results are reported only as colony-forming units per plate, which cannot be compared to outdoor baselines or published health benchmarks. (3) Larger, heavier spores (Cladosporium, Alternaria) are over-represented compared to small spores (Aspergillus, Stachybotrys) that remain airborne longer. (4) AIHA and IICRC guidance does not endorse settled plate sampling as a primary assessment method. IETI uses only calibrated, volume-based sampling methods (Air-O-Cell cassettes with calibrated pumps) to ensure results are defensible, reproducible, and scientifically valid.

## 1.3 Mold Species Reference — The Critical Species

### Aspergillus (Multiple Species/Sections)
**Appearance:** Varies significantly by species. Typically produces powdery, granular colonies in shades of green (A. fumigatus), yellow-green (A. flavus), black (A. niger), or brown. Characterized by flask-shaped vesicles bearing chains of conidia (spores). **Water Activity Required:** Most species require aw ≥ 0.80 (moderately moist substrates). A. versicolor and A. penicillioides are xerophilic, growing at aw as low as 0.73–0.75 (nearly dry conditions). **Mycotoxins Produced:** Aflatoxins B1, B2, G1, G2 (A. flavus, A. parasiticus — IARC Group 1 carcinogens); Ochratoxin A (A. ochraceus, A. westerdijkiae); Gliotoxin (A. fumigatus); Sterigmatocystin (A. versicolor — a potent carcinogen). **Health Effects:** Allergic aspergillosis, asthma exacerbation; in immunocompromised patients: invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (life-threatening); Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA). **Typical Substrates:** Water-damaged drywall, HVAC systems, fibrous insulation, wallpaper. **In Reports:** Reported as Pen/Asp on non-viable spore trap reports; requires viable sampling or ERMI for species-level identification.

### Penicillium (Multiple Species)
**Appearance:** Blue-green to blue powdery colonies; spores produced in chains from brush-like structures (penicilli). **Water Activity Required:** aw 0.78–0.82 depending on species. Among the most common water-damage indicators. **Mycotoxins Produced:** Ochratoxin A (P. verrucosum), Patulin (P. expansum — associated with apple products), Citrinin, Mycophenolic acid. **Health Effects:** Respiratory allergy, asthma exacerbation; some species produce clinically significant mycotoxins. **Typical Substrates:** Drywall, wood, paper products, food. **In Reports:** Reported combined with Aspergillus as "Pen/Asp" on non-viable analysis. Penicillium/Aspergillus is the most commonly elevated group indoors.

### Stachybotrys chartarum ("Black Mold")
**Appearance:** Black to dark greenish-black, slimy colonies; relatively large spores (6–10 × 7–12 µm). **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.94 — requires very wet substrates, specifically cellulose-rich materials that remain wet for extended periods (7–14+ days). This is critically important: Stachybotrys does NOT grow on drywall that got briefly wet and dried. It requires chronically wet cellulose — typically the paper facer of water-saturated drywall, cardboard, or water-logged wood. **Mycotoxins Produced:** Trichothecenes (satratoxins H and G, roridin E, verrucarin J); these are extremely potent mycotoxins with immunosuppressive and cytotoxic effects. Also produces spirocyclins, stachylysin (a hemolysin). **Health Effects:** Pulmonary hemorrhage (documented in infants in the Cleveland cluster studies, though causation remains debated in the literature); chronic fatigue, headache, immune dysregulation in chronically exposed individuals; trichothecene mycotoxins are among the most toxic naturally occurring compounds. **Typical Substrates:** Paper-faced drywall, fiberboard, cardboard, ceiling tiles, wood, paper — all must be continuously or repeatedly wet. NOT typically found on dry surfaces. **In Reports:** Stachybotrys spores are fragile and sticky — they do not readily become airborne in settled dust. Spore trap air sampling frequently misses Stachybotrys even when it is present. ERMI (dust-based PCR) is significantly more sensitive for Stachybotrys than air sampling. When found in air samples, it is always clinically significant regardless of count.

### Chaetomium globosum
**Appearance:** Gray-olive to brown-black; produces distinctive flask-shaped fruiting bodies (perithecia) under microscopy. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.90. **Mycotoxins Produced:** Chaetoglobosins (cytotoxic), chaetomin, sterigmatocystin. **Health Effects:** Associated with respiratory symptoms, skin infections in immunocompromised individuals; chaetoglobosins are cytotoxic at the cellular level. **Typical Substrates:** Paper-faced drywall, wood, canvas — similar niche to Stachybotrys. Finding Chaetomium in dust or samples is a strong indicator of chronic water damage. **In Reports:** A Group 1 ERMI species; its presence in any meaningful quantity is a red flag for water-damaged building materials.

### Cladosporium (C. cladosporioides, C. sphaerospermum, C. herbarum)
**Appearance:** Olive-green to black powdery colonies; chains of ellipsoidal spores with distinct scars (disjunctors). Very common both indoors and outdoors. **Water Activity Required:** C. cladosporioides aw ≥ 0.86; C. sphaerospermum aw ≥ 0.84. C. sphaerospermum is a water-damage indicator (Group 1 ERMI); C. cladosporioides is a common outdoor species (Group 2 ERMI). **Mycotoxins:** No major clinically significant mycotoxins. **Health Effects:** Primary concern is allergic disease — Cladosporium is among the most common causes of fungal allergy in North America. Asthma exacerbation. **Typical Substrates:** Exterior surfaces, window frames, bathroom grout, any surface with intermittent moisture. **In Reports:** Almost always present in both indoor and outdoor samples; elevation of C. sphaerospermum indoors relative to outdoor is significant; C. cladosporioides indoors roughly equal to outdoor is expected.

### Alternaria alternata
**Appearance:** Dark olive-brown colonies; large, multi-celled (muriform) spores with transverse and longitudinal septa — distinctive "club" or "drumstick" shape. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.85. **Mycotoxins:** Alternariol, altenuene, tenuazonic acid — mutagenic potential documented. **Health Effects:** Major airborne allergen, strongly associated with severe asthma attacks (particularly summer/fall outdoors); atopic disease. Indoor elevation suggests specific substrate contamination. **Typical Substrates:** Window frames, HVAC ducts, basement walls, any damp cellulosic surface. **In Reports:** Primarily an outdoor species; significant indoor elevation above outdoor baseline warrants source investigation.

### Fusarium (Multiple Species)
**Appearance:** White to pink to purple colonies; distinctive curved, boat-shaped macroconidia. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.90. **Mycotoxins Produced:** Deoxynivalenol (DON/vomitoxin), fumonisins, zearalenone, trichothecenes — particularly clinically significant. **Health Effects:** Eye infections (particularly contact lens users); sinusitis; in immunocompromised patients can cause invasive infection. Fumonisins are classified as IARC Group 2B possible carcinogens. **Typical Substrates:** Wet or flood-damaged materials; soil; HVAC systems; fibrous insulation. **In Reports:** Indoor elevation is clinically significant, especially in immunocompromised occupant situations.

### Trichoderma (T. viride, T. harzianum)
**Appearance:** Green colonies with white margins; rapid growing. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.91. **Mycotoxins:** Trichothecenes in some species (Trichoderma aggressivum). **Health Effects:** Documented cause of infection in immunocompromised patients; allergen; associated with bioaerosol symptoms. **Typical Substrates:** Water-damaged wood, drywall, soil-contaminated substrates. **In Reports:** Group 1 ERMI species; indoor elevation is a water-damage indicator.

### Ulocladium (U. botrytis, U. chartarum)
**Appearance:** Dark olive to black colonies; spores superficially resemble Alternaria but smaller. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.89. **Mycotoxins:** Limited documentation. **Health Effects:** Allergen; respiratory irritation. **Typical Substrates:** Paper-faced drywall, wood, other cellulosic materials — requires persistently wet conditions. **In Reports:** A water-damage indicator species; elevation relative to outdoor baseline is significant.

### Wallemia sebi
**Appearance:** Yellowish-brown dry colonies; very small, barrel-shaped spores. Grows extremely slowly. **Water Activity Required:** aw as low as 0.69 — one of the most xerophilic (drought-tolerant) molds known. Grows in very dry, dusty conditions. **Mycotoxins:** Walleminol, wallimidione — bioactive compounds, clinical significance being studied. **Health Effects:** Associated with farmer's lung (hypersensitivity pneumonitis); a Group 1 HERTSMI-2 species in the Shoemaker CIRS protocol. **Typical Substrates:** Dried food, dust, wall cavities with only moderate moisture levels — uniquely problematic because it grows where other molds cannot. **In Reports:** Finding Wallemia sebi in ERMI is significant because it can grow in relatively dry environments and contributes to HERTSMI-2 score.

### Aureobasidium pullulans
**Appearance:** Initially white, rapidly turning black with age; yeast-like cells and hyphae. **Water Activity Required:** aw ≥ 0.89. **Mycotoxins:** Aureobasidin (antifungal compound, not primarily a human toxin). **Health Effects:** Allergen; hypersensitivity pneumonitis (especially in woodworkers); rare invasive disease in immunocompromised. **Typical Substrates:** Painted surfaces, window frames, sealants, wood — particularly common on exterior window frames that experience rain wetting and drying cycles. **In Reports:** Common finding on window frame surface samples; moderate significance.

### Serpula lacrymans ("True Dry Rot")
**Appearance:** White mycelial cords (rhizomorphs) that can travel meters across inorganic substrates; orange-brown fruiting bodies; produces distinctive "cuboidal cracking" of wood. **Water Activity Required:** aw 0.97–0.98 — requires near-saturation but is uniquely capable of transporting its own water via mycelial cords. **Mycotoxins:** None clinically documented. **Health Effects:** Primarily a building pathogen rather than a direct human health threat at typical exposure levels; associated with significant structural damage. **Typical Substrates:** Softwood construction lumber; particularly problematic in older homes and under floor structures in contact with soil moisture. **In Reports:** Finding Serpula in a building sample indicates severe, chronic moisture intrusion and significant structural degradation. Urgent remediation required.

**Standard Turnaround:** 3–5 business days from sample receipt at lab.
**Rush Turnaround:** 24–48 hours from sample receipt; available at additional cost; used when occupant health is at immediate risk or real estate deadlines require rapid results.

### Reading Air Sample Results
Lab results for Air-O-Cell/spore trap analysis are reported in raw spore counts and calculated spores per cubic meter (spores/m³). The lab reports each spore type (Pen/Asp, Cladosporium, Stachybotrys, Ascospores, Basidiospores, etc.) separately.

**Significant Elevation Thresholds:**
- **General principle:** Indoor air levels greater than 1.5× outdoor baseline for any spore type warrants further investigation and notation in the report. This is not a regulatory standard — it is a professional judgment guideline widely used in the IEP (Indoor Environmental Professional) community.
- **Penicillium/Aspergillus (Pen/Asp):** Any significant indoor elevation above the outdoor baseline is clinically important, even if the absolute number seems low, because many Aspergillus and Penicillium species are obligate indicators of indoor moisture problems (they are not typically elevated outdoors in the same ratio as indoors when a moisture source is present). A Pen/Asp ratio of indoor/outdoor greater than 1.5–2.0 in the absence of explanatory outdoor sources is flagged.
- **Stachybotrys:** Any detected Stachybotrys spores in an indoor air sample are clinically significant. There is no established "background" level for Stachybotrys in indoor air — its presence indicates active or recent water damage.
- **Total spore counts:** Indoor total counts above 10,000–15,000 spores/m³ generally indicate a significant contamination issue. Counts above 50,000 spores/m³ indicate severe contamination.

**IICRC S520 Standard:** IETI's remediation recommendations are based on the IICRC S520 "Standard for Professional Mold Remediation" (current edition). This standard defines contamination conditions (Condition 1/2/3), delineates remediation scope, and specifies clearance criteria for post-remediation verification.

**Post-Remediation Verification (Clearance) Protocol:**
After mold remediation is complete and the work area has been cleaned, HEPA vacuumed, and containment is still in place, IETI conducts independent clearance testing. Clearance criteria (per IICRC S520):
1. No visible mold growth remains.
2. No elevated moisture readings in remediated areas.
3. Post-remediation air samples from the work area must not exceed Condition 1 (normal/background) levels. Post-remediation air samples are compared to outdoor baseline — the remediated area should have air quality comparable to or better than outdoors.
4. Containment is released only after clearance is confirmed.
IETI performs clearance testing as an independent third party — we do not do remediation, eliminating any conflict of interest.

## 1.5 Health Effects of Mold Exposure

### Mycotoxins
Mycotoxins are secondary metabolites produced by certain mold species under specific environmental conditions (not all molds produce mycotoxins, and mold spores do not always contain mycotoxins). Relevant mycotoxins:

- **Trichothecenes (Stachybotrys, Fusarium, Trichoderma):** A class of over 150 sesquiterpenoid compounds. Satratoxins (produced by Stachybotrys) are among the most potent. Mechanism of toxicity: inhibit protein synthesis at the ribosomal level; potently immunosuppressive; can cross the blood-brain barrier. The nose-to-brain transport pathway (olfactory neurons) may be particularly significant for inhaled trichothecenes.
- **Aflatoxins (Aspergillus flavus, A. parasiticus):** IARC Group 1 confirmed human carcinogens. Aflatoxin B1 is the most potent naturally occurring hepatocarcinogen known. Primary concern in food contamination (corn, peanuts, tree nuts), but relevant in water-damaged buildings if A. flavus is identified.
- **Ochratoxin A (Aspergillus ochraceus, Penicillium verrucosum):** Nephrotoxic (damages kidneys); immunosuppressive; classified IARC Group 2B possible carcinogen. Has been measured in the urine of water-damaged building occupants.
- **Gliotoxin (Aspergillus fumigatus):** Potent immunosuppressant that inhibits phagocytosis by macrophages and neutrophils; promotes fungal survival in the lung.
- **Patulin (Penicillium expansum, Aspergillus clavatus):** Genotoxic, immunotoxic; primarily a concern in apple juice products but produced by building-related species.

### Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (HP)
HP is an immune-mediated inflammatory lung disease caused by repeated inhalation of organic antigens, including fungal spores and fragments. Acute HP: onset within 4–8 hours of exposure, with fever, chills, cough, shortness of breath, myalgia. Symptoms resolve within 12–24 hours of leaving the contaminated environment — this pattern is diagnostically significant. Subacute and chronic HP develop with lower-level repeated exposure; chronic HP can result in irreversible pulmonary fibrosis. Classic occupational HP examples include Farmer's Lung (thermophilic actinomycetes in moldy hay) and Hot Tub Lung (Mycobacterium avium complex in aerosolized spa water). In buildings: elevated mold concentrations from HVAC contamination or flooded materials are documented HP triggers.

### CIRS (Chronic Inflammatory Response Syndrome)
CIRS is a multi-system, multi-symptom chronic illness diagnosed using the protocol developed by Dr. Ritchie Shoemaker (Surviving Mold). CIRS is proposed to result in genetically susceptible individuals (specific HLA-DR/DQ genotypes that cannot effectively clear biotoxins) following exposure to water-damaged buildings. Key elements of the Shoemaker protocol:
- **HLA-DR genotyping:** Specific HLA-DR/DQ genotypes are associated with inability to mount effective immune responses to biotoxins. HLA 4-3-53, 11-3-52B, and 14-5-52B (among others) are considered "dreaded" genotypes with poor prognosis if exposure continues. Approximately 25% of the population carries a susceptible genotype.
- **Visual Contrast Sensitivity (VCS) test:** A validated neurotoxicity screening tool; patients with biotoxin illness commonly fail VCS.
- **Lab biomarkers:** MSH (Melanocyte-Stimulating Hormone) — typically low in CIRS; TGF-beta-1 — typically elevated; MMP-9 (Matrix Metalloproteinase-9) — typically elevated; C4a complement — typically elevated; VEGF — variable; ADH/osmolality. No single biomarker is definitive; the pattern of abnormalities is diagnostic.
- **HERTSMI-2 and ERMI** are the preferred environmental tests for CIRS assessment because dust-based PCR is more sensitive for the key organisms (Stachybotrys, Chaetomium, Wallemia) than air sampling.
- IETI's role: we provide the environmental data (ERMI, HERTSMI-2, thorough inspection) that the treating physician uses in context with clinical findings. We do not diagnose CIRS — that is a medical determination.

### Reactive Airways Disease and Asthma
Mold exposure is a well-documented trigger for occupational and non-occupational asthma. The ARIA (Allergic Rhinitis and Its Impact on Asthma) classification and GINA (Global Initiative for Asthma) guidelines both recognize fungal sensitization as a significant asthma driver. Sensitization to Alternaria and Cladosporium is particularly associated with severe asthma attacks. Occupational asthma has been documented in workers exposed to Aspergillus and Penicillium in agricultural and food processing settings.

### Immunocompromised Patients
Patients with hematologic malignancies (leukemia, lymphoma), solid organ transplant recipients on immunosuppressive therapy, patients receiving corticosteroid therapy, and HIV/AIDS patients with low CD4 counts are at heightened risk for invasive fungal disease. Aspergillus fumigatus causes the majority of invasive mold infections in immunocompromised patients. Standard residential Aspergillus concentrations that are tolerable for immunocompetent individuals can be life-threatening for these patients. IETI recommends extremely conservative thresholds and more frequent retesting for buildings housing immunocompromised occupants.

### Infants and Elderly
Infants under 12 months have immature immune systems and respiratory tracts with proportionally higher tidal volume per body weight — they inhale more air per kilogram than adults. Elderly individuals often have pre-existing respiratory or cardiac conditions and immune senescence. Both populations are disproportionately affected by the same mold concentrations that might produce only minor symptoms in healthy adults. IETI factors these considerations into interpretation and recommendations.

# SECTION 2: AIR QUALITY TESTING — COMPLETE TECHNICAL REFERENCE

## 2.1 Full Parameter List with Reference Standards

### VOCs — Total (TVOC)
Total Volatile Organic Compounds measured as a sum of all detected VOC concentrations. **WHO guidance:** 400 µg/m³ is the comfort threshold; 400–2,000 µg/m³ is the "multifactorial exposure" range where symptoms become likely in sensitive individuals; 2,000–25,000 µg/m³ produces discomfort and symptoms in most people; above 25,000 µg/m³ is the toxic range. Note: TVOC is an imprecise metric — two buildings with the same TVOC may have very different risk profiles depending on which specific compounds compose that total. IETI always pursues speciated VOC analysis (individual compound identification) rather than relying on TVOC alone.

### Individual VOCs of Critical Importance

**Benzene:** IARC Group 1 confirmed human carcinogen (leukemia); no safe level of exposure — any detectable indoor concentration is concerning. Sources: vehicle exhaust (attached garages), tobacco smoke, off-gassing from some paints and adhesives, contaminated groundwater (relevant in some Madison/Nashville-area properties). OSHA PEL: 1 ppm (8-hr TWA); ACGIH TLV: 0.5 ppm. WHO recommends reducing exposure as low as possible.

**Formaldehyde:** IARC Group 1 carcinogen (nasopharyngeal cancer, leukemia). Also a potent mucous membrane irritant at low concentrations. **WHO indoor air quality guideline: 0.10 mg/m³ (0.08 ppm) 30-minute average** — this is the key reference number. **OSHA PEL: 0.75 ppm (8-hr TWA); OSHA STEL: 2 ppm**. At 0.1 ppm: eye and throat irritation begins in sensitive individuals. At 0.5 ppm: moderate irritation in most people. At 1 ppm+: significant irritation. Primary indoor sources: urea-formaldehyde (UF) resin in engineered wood products (MDF, particleboard, hardwood plywood), UF-based insulation (UFFI), certain paints and finishes.

**Toluene:** Central nervous system depressant at high levels; reproductive toxicant (documented fetal solvent syndrome). OSHA TWA: 200 ppm; ACGIH TLV-TWA: 20 ppm. Sources: paints, adhesives, nail polish, gasoline.

**Xylene (o-, m-, p-isomers):** Neurological effects at high concentrations. OSHA TWA: 100 ppm. Sources: paints, varnishes, rust preventers.

**Ethylbenzene:** IARC Group 2B possible carcinogen. OSHA TWA: 100 ppm. Sources: gasoline, paints.

**Styrene:** IARC Group 2A probable carcinogen. OSHA TWA: 100 ppm. Sources: fiberglass installation, polystyrene products, some adhesives.

**Acetaldehyde:** IARC Group 1 carcinogen (sufficient evidence in animals, limited in humans). Sources: tobacco smoke, wood combustion, off-gassing from some finishes and composites.

**Acrolein:** Potent respiratory irritant; no established safe indoor level. Sources: cooking fats (overheated), tobacco smoke, fire smoke.

### Radon
**EPA Action Level: 4 pCi/L.** At 4 pCi/L, EPA estimates approximately 7 lung cancer deaths per 1,000 non-smokers over a lifetime of exposure (risk is significantly higher for smokers). **WHO recommended reference level: 2.7 pCi/L (100 Bq/m³).** EPA recommends considering mitigation at 2–4 pCi/L and mitigation at ≥4 pCi/L. Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the US, responsible for approximately 21,000 deaths per year.

**Madison, WI context:** Wisconsin has significant radon concerns particularly in areas overlying granite bedrock and glacial till. Wisconsin DHFS radon testing data shows multiple Dane County zip codes with median indoor radon levels above 4 pCi/L. Radon testing is strongly recommended for all Madison-area homes, especially those with basements.

**Nashville, TN context:** Tennessee has moderate radon potential. The Tennessee Department of Environment and Conservation (TDEC) recommends testing all homes below the third floor. Middle Tennessee karst geology can create localized elevated radon zones.

**Measurement Methods:**
- **Electret Ion Chambers (E-PERM system):** Short-term (48-hour minimum) or long-term passive devices. Teflon electret disk records ionization from radon progeny. Sent to lab for reading. Accurate and widely used.
- **Alpha Track Detectors (Accustar AT100, RadStar):** Long-term devices (90 days to 1 year). Plastic film records alpha particle tracks; lab reads track density. Most accurate for annual average exposure assessment.
- **CRM — Continuous Radon Monitors (Sun Nuclear 1027, Rad7):** Real-time, hour-by-hour radon measurement. Required for real estate transaction testing (EPA/AARST protocols require closed-house conditions for 12 hours minimum, 48-hour measurement). Sun Nuclear 1027 is the industry standard CRM for real estate testing — tamper-evident, records temperature, humidity, and barometric pressure to detect test interference.

### Carbon Monoxide (CO)
**OSHA PEL: 50 ppm (8-hr TWA).** NIOSH IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health): 1,200 ppm. **Symptom thresholds:** 70 ppm — headache, fatigue, nausea after 2–3 hours; 150–200 ppm — headache, dizziness, disorientation after 2–3 hours; 400 ppm — life-threatening within 3 hours; 800 ppm — death within 2–3 hours; 1,600 ppm — death within 1 hour. Sources: gas appliances (furnaces, water heaters, ranges), attached garages, backdrafting chimneys, generators. CO is odorless and colorless — detector alarms are set at 70 ppm for 1–4 hours, which means sub-alarm chronic exposures at 35–69 ppm may not trigger alarms but are health-relevant. IETI measures CO with direct-read instruments and logs concentrations during the inspection.

### Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)
**Outdoor baseline: approximately 420–425 ppm** (current atmospheric concentration as of 2025–2026). CO₂ is used as a surrogate marker for occupant-generated bioeffluents and general ventilation adequacy. **ASHRAE 62.1 reference levels:** Generally, keeping CO₂ below 700 ppm above outdoor baseline is considered adequate ventilation for most occupancies. **Interpretation thresholds:** 400–800 ppm — well ventilated; 800–1,000 ppm — adequate ventilation, some occupants may notice stuffiness; 1,000–2,000 ppm — drowsiness and reduced productivity documented; above 2,000 ppm — cognitive impairment documented (decision-making, response times impaired); above 5,000 ppm — OSHA PEL threshold for occupational settings. CO₂ itself at typical indoor levels is not a primary health hazard — its value is as a ventilation indicator. High CO₂ in a space means occupant bioeffluents are not being diluted, which implies other pollutants may also be elevated.

### Particulate Matter (PM)

**PM2.5 (particles ≤ 2.5 µm diameter):**
- EPA NAAQS 24-hour standard: 35 µg/m³
- EPA NAAQS annual standard: 12 µg/m³ (primary); 15 µg/m³ (secondary)
- WHO 2021 Global Air Quality Guidelines: 15 µg/m³ annual; 25 µg/m³ 24-hour average — these are stricter than current EPA standards
- PM2.5 penetrates to alveolar regions of the lung; associated with cardiovascular disease, lung cancer, premature mortality

**PM10 (particles ≤ 10 µm diameter):**
- EPA NAAQS 24-hour standard: 150 µg/m³
- WHO 2021: 45 µg/m³ 24-hour; 15 µg/m³ annual

**PM1.0 (particles ≤ 1.0 µm):** Not currently regulated but captured by some direct-read instruments; these ultrafine particles penetrate deepest into lung tissue.

### Allergens (Dust and Surface Testing)
- **Dust mite allergen Der p1 (Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus) and Der f1 (D. farinae):** Measured in floor dust via ELISA. **Action levels: 2 µg/g dust** = sensitization risk threshold; **10 µg/g dust** = acute symptom threshold in sensitized individuals. Dust mites require RH above 50% and temperatures 65–80°F — HVAC humidity control is the primary mitigation strategy.
- **Cat allergen Fel d1:** Highly persistent — remains elevated in homes 6+ months after cat removal. Action level for sensitive individuals: 8 µg/g. Transports readily on clothing.
- **Dog allergen Can f1:** Less persistent than cat allergen. Measurable in homes without dogs due to secondary transfer.
- **Cockroach allergen Bla g2 (German cockroach):** Strong association with asthma in urban environments. Action level: 0.1–0.2 µg/g dust in bedrooms.

### Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂)
**WHO annual guideline: 40 µg/m³.** Primary indoor source: unvented or poorly vented gas appliances (gas ranges are the most significant source of NO₂ in residential settings). Cooking on a gas range can produce indoor NO₂ peaks of 200–400+ µg/m³. NO₂ irritates the respiratory tract and increases susceptibility to respiratory infections.

### Ozone (O₃)
**EPA 8-hour standard: 0.070 ppm (70 ppb).** Primary indoor sources: ozone-generating air purifiers (which IETI strongly advises against — these devices produce ozone at levels that can be irritating and harmful), some laser printers and photocopiers. Ozone is a potent lung irritant; reacts with VOCs to form secondary pollutants including formaldehyde and fine particles.

## 2.3 Sick Building Syndrome Protocol

**NIOSH Definition and Investigation Protocol:** NIOSH defines Sick Building Syndrome (SBS) as a situation where building occupants experience acute health and comfort effects that appear to be linked to time spent in a building but no specific illness or cause can be identified. This is distinguished from "Building-Related Illness" (BRI) where a specific cause is identified (e.g., Legionella, hypersensitivity pneumonitis). NIOSH's Health Hazard Evaluation (HHE) program has established an investigation protocol that IETI follows: (1) occupant symptom survey; (2) HVAC system inspection and measurement; (3) CO₂ and IAQ parameter monitoring; (4) VOC and specific contaminant sampling; (5) source identification.

**Diagnostic Pattern for SBS:** Occupants report symptoms (headache, eye/nose/throat irritation, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, skin irritation) during building occupancy that improve or resolve when away from the building (evenings, weekends, vacations). This temporal relationship is the most important diagnostic feature.

**ASHRAE 62.1 Ventilation Requirements:**
- **Office spaces:** 5 CFM/person outdoor air + 0.06 CFM/ft² area component (total typically ~17–20 CFM/person)
- **Minimum outdoor air:** 7.5 CFM/person for most commercial occupancies
- **Classroom:** 10 CFM/person + 0.12 CFM/ft²
- **Healthcare:** Substantially higher, with specific pressurization and filtration requirements
- CO₂ measurements map directly to ventilation adequacy — if CO₂ is consistently 800–1,200 ppm above outdoor background with normal occupancy, ventilation is likely below ASHRAE 62.1 requirements.

**HVAC Assessment Protocol:**
IETI inspects: air handling unit (AHU) condition (coil cleanliness, drain pan standing water — primary Legionella and mold reservoir); supply and return duct condition and cleanliness; filter condition and MERV rating (MERV 8 is minimum recommended for occupied commercial spaces; MERV 13 is preferred for PM2.5 capture; HEPA = MERV 17+); humidifier condition (reservoir humidifiers are high-risk for microbial amplification); outdoor air intake location (proximity to exhaust vents, loading docks, parking garages, cooling towers); VAV (variable air volume) box functionality — malfunctioning VAVs can create zones of severe under-ventilation.

## 2.4 New Construction Off-Gassing

**Off-Gassing Timelines by Material:**
- **Latex paint:** Peak emissions in first 24–72 hours; returns to background levels within 1–4 weeks.
- **Oil-based paint and stains:** Off-gassing can persist 30–60+ days.
- **Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP) and Vinyl Tile flooring:** Peak VOC emissions (particularly plasticizers including phthalates, and various solvents from manufacturing) in weeks 1–4; emissions typically drop significantly by week 8–12 but some low-level off-gassing continues for months.
- **Carpet and carpet adhesive:** Peak emissions weeks 1–3; 4-PC (4-phenylcyclohexene) is the characteristic "new carpet smell" compound; styrene and 4-vinylcyclohexene are also common carpet off-gassing VOCs. Formaldehyde from latex binders.
- **Pressed wood products (MDF, particleboard, hardwood plywood) and furniture:** Urea-formaldehyde resin off-gassing is highest initially and declines exponentially, but continues at lower levels for years. UF resins hydrolyze in humid conditions, re-releasing formaldehyde when RH increases.
- **Flooring adhesives (solvent-based):** Peak emissions week 1–8; hexane, cyclohexane, aromatic hydrocarbons typical.

**Formaldehyde Regulatory Limits in Engineered Wood:**
Under the EPA TSCA Title VI rule (implementing CARB Phase 2 standards, now federal law):
- **Hardwood plywood (HWPW):** 0.05 ppm formaldehyde emission limit
- **Medium-density fiberboard (MDF):** 0.11 ppm (full thickness); 0.13 ppm (thin MDF)
- **Particleboard:** 0.09 ppm
- **CARB Phase 2** set these limits; EPA TSCA Title VI made them nationally applicable. Products should carry CARB Phase 2 / TSCA Title VI compliance labels.

**Optimal Testing Timing:**
IETI recommends testing 7–30 days after occupancy or construction completion. Prior to testing: building should have been in "closed building conditions" — all windows and doors closed, HVAC operating normally — for a minimum of 12 hours before sampling. This is per EPA sampling protocol and prevents the artificial dilution of indoor concentrations by outside air ventilation.

**Certification Context:**
- **GREENGUARD Gold (UL Environment):** Certifies products meet indoor air quality standards including formaldehyde. GREENGUARD Gold (formerly Children & Schools) has the most stringent thresholds, suitable for schools and healthcare.
- **FloorScore (SCS Global Services):** Tests resilient flooring and adhesives to CA Section 01350 — the most stringent state VOC emission standard. A FloorScore-certified floor does not eliminate off-gassing concerns, but it should meet aggressive emission thresholds.
- IETI notes to clients: even certified products off-gas in new construction — certification means products meet specific thresholds when new, but the combination of all certified products in a newly constructed/renovated space can still produce elevated aggregate TVOC.

# SECTION 3: EMF TESTING — COMPLETE TECHNICAL REFERENCE

## 3.1 EMF Types and Measurement Standards

### ELF Magnetic Fields (Extremely Low Frequency, 50/60 Hz)
**Units:** Measured in milligauss (mG) or microtesla (µT). Conversion: 1 mG = 0.1 µT (or equivalently, 10 mG = 1 µT).

**Regulatory Standards:**
- **ICNIRP (International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection) 2010 Guidelines:** 2,000 mG (200 µT) occupational; 1,000 mG (100 µT) general public reference level (at 60 Hz). These limits are set to prevent acute thermal and neurostimulation effects — not chronic exposure.
- **FCC:** Follows ICNIRP for ELF.

**Precautionary Standards (Building Biology SBM-2015):**
Building Biology (Institut für Baubiologie und Nachhaltigkeit — IBN) provides precautionary guidelines based on the Precautionary Principle and incorporating research on chronic low-level biological effects:
- **< 0.2 mG:** No concern (precautionary — appropriate sleeping area target)
- **0.2–1.0 mG:** Slight concern (precautionary sleeping area)
- **1.0–5.0 mG:** Severe concern
- **> 5.0 mG:** Extreme concern

**WHO IARC Classification:** In 2002, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified ELF magnetic fields as **Group 2B — possible human carcinogen**, based primarily on epidemiological studies showing a statistical association between childhood leukemia and residential ELF magnetic field exposure above 4 mG (0.4 µT). This classification does not confirm causation but does justify precautionary approaches.

### ELF Electric Fields (50/60 Hz)
**Units:** Volts per meter (V/m).

**Building Biology SBM-2015 Guidelines:**
- **< 1 V/m:** No concern (precautionary sleeping area)
- **1–5 V/m:** Slight concern
- **5–50 V/m:** Severe concern
- **> 50 V/m:** Extreme concern

Electric fields are produced by energized wiring whether or not current is flowing. They are effectively blocked by earthed (grounded) conductors. In buildings with metal-conduit wiring or grounded romex, electric fields from house wiring are significantly attenuated. Wiring in walls near beds can produce electric fields in the 5–50 V/m range at the bed — reducing by unplugging lamps and devices, or installing switched-neutral circuits.

### RF/Microwave Fields (Radiofrequency, 100 kHz – 300 GHz)
**Units:** Microvolts per meter (µV/m) for field strength, or microwatts per square meter (µW/m²) for power density. The conversion: Power density (µW/m²) = E² / 3,770 (where E is in V/m).

**Regulatory Standards:**
- **FCC (USA) limits:** Frequency-dependent. At 900 MHz (cellular): 600 µW/cm² (= 6,000,000 µW/m²) — occupational; 100 µW/cm² (= 1,000,000 µW/m²) public. Note: these limits are based on a 6-minute average and primarily address thermal heating effects.
- **ICNIRP 2020 limits:** At 900 MHz: 22.5 W/m² (= 22,500,000 µW/m²) general public (peak spatial). For time-averaged: 4.5 W/m² (4,500,000 µW/m²). Widely used internationally.

**Precautionary Standards (Building Biology SBM-2015):**
These are orders of magnitude more conservative than ICNIRP/FCC:
- **< 0.1 µW/m²:** No concern (precautionary sleeping area)
- **0.1–10 µW/m²:** Slight concern
- **10–1,000 µW/m²:** Severe concern
- **> 1,000 µW/m²:** Extreme concern
These guidelines are based on biological effect studies at sub-thermal levels and the Precautionary Principle. They are not regulatory standards in the US but are used by Building Biology professionals.

### Dirty Electricity (High-Frequency Voltage Transients on Power Lines)
**Definition:** High-frequency (kHz range) electrical noise superimposed on the standard 60 Hz power line waveform. Generated by: switching power supplies (nearly all modern electronics), variable-speed motors, dimmer switches, compact fluorescent lights (CFLs), solar inverters, and some smart meter communication systems.

**Units:** Graham-Stetzer (GS) units — a proprietary unit measuring the microsurge content of the power line. Developed by Dr. Sam Milham and Dave Stetzer.

**Stetzer Threshold:** The Stetzer Institute recommends maintaining GS units below 50 in occupied spaces. Average US home: 50–150+ GS units in many rooms. Schools with extensive electronics: often 200–500+ GS units.

**Greenwave meter:** A competing dirty electricity meter that also measures in a proprietary unit; Greenwave and Stetzer meters are calibrated differently and produce different numbers from the same power line — both are relative instruments for comparing pre/post filter performance, not absolute scientific measurements.

### Ground Current / Stray Voltage
**Definition:** Net current flowing on the neutral/ground conductors of the building's electrical system, or returning to the utility substation through conductive paths (plumbing, building structure, earth). In areas with older electrical infrastructure or certain wiring configurations, measurable net current can produce magnetic fields throughout a building unrelated to specific circuits.

**Measurement:** Clamp-type AC current meter placed around the neutral conductor at the service entrance, around water pipes, or around clusters of wiring. Net current above 1 ampere is unusual; high net current from utility distribution lines can produce ambient 60 Hz magnetic fields in buildings that cannot be mitigated without utility cooperation.

## 3.3 Common EMF Sources and Typical Levels

- **Smart Meters:** Typically communicate in the 902–928 MHz ISM band. Pulse frequency varies by utility — some meters pulse every 4 seconds (data logging), others every 15–90 seconds for transmission to utility network. Peak power: typically 1–2 watts EIRP. Measured levels at 3 feet from the exterior face of meter: typically 50–1,000+ µW/m². At 10 feet through a wall: typically 5–100 µW/m² (wall attenuation and distance). Some meters have configurable pulse rates; utility companies can sometimes reduce transmission frequency on request.

- **WiFi Router (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz):** At 3 feet from a typical residential router: 500–5,000 µW/m² (higher for dual-band simultaneous transmission). Levels decrease with the inverse square law — doubling distance reduces power density by 75%. At 10 feet: 50–500 µW/m². 5 GHz WiFi does not penetrate walls as well as 2.4 GHz — more rapid signal attenuation. WiFi signals are pulsed (not constant) — beacon frames transmit every 100 ms even without data transfer.

- **Cell Tower:** Highly variable depending on distance, antenna height, directionality, and whether 2G/3G/4G LTE/5G technology. Typical measured levels at 50–200 meters from a macrocell tower: 1–1,000 µW/m². Buildings attenuate signals by 5–30 dB depending on construction materials.

- **5G Small Cells (Two Frequency Categories):**
  - **Sub-6 GHz 5G (600 MHz, 700 MHz, 2.5 GHz, 3.5 GHz):** Propagation similar to existing LTE; penetrates buildings. Typical power levels similar to 4G LTE small cells. This is the majority of deployed 5G as of 2025–2026.
  - **mmWave 5G (24–39 GHz, primarily 28 GHz and 39 GHz):** Very short range (100–200 meters), requires line of sight, easily blocked by windows (particularly Low-E glass) and walls. Indoor levels from outdoor mmWave sources are generally near zero. Primary exposure scenarios are direct proximity to outdoor small cell antennas.

- **Power Lines:**
  - **Transmission lines (115–765 kV):** Very high magnetic fields directly beneath right-of-way; 50–100+ mG at the edge of ROW. Drops rapidly with distance.
  - **Distribution lines (7.2–34.5 kV):** 10–20 mG at 10 feet; drops to 2–5 mG at 25 feet; approaches background (0.2–0.5 mG) at 50–100 feet.
  - **Service drop to house:** 1–5 mG at 3 feet, negligible at 10 feet.

- **Wiring Errors (Net Current/Neutral-Ground Bonding Issues):** When the neutral conductor is improperly bonded to ground at a sub-panel, or when current returns to the service panel via unintended paths (ground conductors, conduit, water piping), the net current on house circuits is non-zero, creating persistent 60 Hz magnetic fields throughout the building — often along the floor and wall surfaces. This can produce 5–50 mG throughout a room even when no specific appliance is running. Identified by clamp-meter measurement of net current on house circuits. Corrected only by a licensed electrician.

## 3.4 Building Biology Assessment Protocol (SBM-2015 Standard)

IETI's EMF assessments follow the SBM-2015 (Standard der Baubiologischen Messtechnik) methodology developed by the Institut für Baubiologie, Germany.

**Assessment Priority:**
Sleeping areas receive the highest priority because the body's circadian repair and immune processes occur during sleep, and occupants spend 6–9 hours per night in these locations — the highest cumulative exposure scenario.

**Protocol Steps:**
1. **Client intake:** History of symptoms, concerns, building age, major renovations, proximity to infrastructure (cell towers, power lines, transformer vaults).
2. **ELF Magnetic Field Room-by-Room Survey:** Three-axis AC magnetic field measurements in each room; measurements are repeated with appliances off (background wiring fields) and on (contribution from specific appliances). Results plotted on floor plan map. Particular attention to bedrooms — all six sides of the sleeping position (head, foot, left, right, floor, ceiling) are measured.
3. **ELF Electric Field Assessment:** Measurements at sleeping positions with all circuits energized vs. circuits serving that room de-energized (circuit breaker off). Identifies whether bedroom wiring contributes significant electric fields.
4. **RF/Microwave Survey:** Room-by-room measurements with windows open and closed; identification of signal sources using spectrum analyzer; 24-hour data logging deployment in bedroom if initial readings indicate elevated RF.
5. **Dirty Electricity Baseline:** GS unit readings at multiple outlets in each room; baseline established before any filter installation.
6. **Ground Current Assessment:** Clamp meter measurement at service entry and around plumbing.
7. **Reporting:** Results mapped to SBM-2015 categories (no concern, slight, severe, extreme) for each field type in each room. Prioritized recommendations provided.

**Mitigation Recommendations:**
- **RF shielding:** YShield HSF54 or HSF64 carbon/graphite-based shielding paint (attenuation 30–35 dB at 1 GHz); applied to walls/ceiling of sleeping area and earthed (grounded). Low-EMF sleeping canopies (Naturell or similar shielding fabric). Low-E glass has limited RF blocking (varies significantly by product). Shielding must be carefully designed to avoid creating a "Faraday cage" that causes devices inside to boost transmit power.
- **Dirty electricity mitigation:** Greenwave or Stetzerizer plug-in filters installed at outlets throughout the building (particularly near sleeping areas). Pre- and post-filter GS measurements confirm effectiveness. One caveat: some filters interact unfavorably with certain appliances — professional assessment needed.
- **ELF magnetic field mitigation:** For wiring errors — licensed electrician to correct net current issue. Distance is the primary mitigation for appliances (inverse square law). For power line fields — relocation of sleeping area may be necessary if fields are above SBM-2015 concern levels.
- **RF source management:** Router timer (off during sleep hours), wired ethernet instead of WiFi for computers, corded telephone, Airplane Mode for mobile devices during sleep.

# SECTION 4: CHEMICAL & ADDITIONAL TESTING — COMPLETE TECHNICAL REFERENCE

## 4.1 Asbestos

### Materials at Risk (ACM — Asbestos-Containing Materials)
Asbestos was widely used in building materials through the late 1970s and into the early 1980s in some applications. Materials that may contain asbestos in buildings constructed before 1980–1985:
- **9" × 9" vinyl floor tiles:** Very high prevalence of asbestos (up to 80% chrysotile by weight in some products). The 9" × 9" format is the most reliable visual indicator; 12" × 12" post-1970 tiles may also contain asbestos.
- **Black cutback mastic floor tile adhesive:** Commonly contains asbestos; found beneath vinyl tile, vinyl composition tile, and sheet vinyl. May contain asbestos even in buildings constructed in the mid-1980s.
- **Acoustical ceiling tiles (drop ceilings):** Variable; textured spray-on and lay-in tiles from the 1960s–70s may contain asbestos.
- **Popcorn/textured spray ceilings (pre-1980):** EPA banned spray-on surfacing materials containing >1% asbestos in 1978 for most uses, but existing applied materials were not required to be removed. Products manufactured prior to the ban frequently contain asbestos.
- **Pipe and boiler insulation:** Amosite (brown) and chrysotile asbestos were widely used. Pre-1970s pipe insulation is extremely high-risk.
- **Roofing felt and shingles:** Asbestos-cement roofing (Transite panels, corrugated asbestos cement) widely used in commercial buildings.
- **Transite pipe:** Asbestos-cement pipe used for flue liners, HVAC ducts, and sewer lines.
- **Textured paint (joint compound):** Some spackling compounds, joint compounds, and textured wall coatings contained asbestos chrysotile fibers through the early 1980s.
- **Vermiculite insulation (Zonolite):** Mined primarily from Libby, Montana, Zonolite vermiculite insulation is contaminated with tremolite asbestos (a particularly toxic amphibole fiber). Estimated 35 million US homes have Zonolite attic insulation.

### Sampling and Analysis Methods
- **PLM (Polarized Light Microscopy):** Standard method for bulk material analysis per EPA 600/R-93/116. Sample prepared and examined under polarized light; asbestos fibers identified by birefringence, morphology, and extinction angle. Results reported as percent asbestos by visual area estimation. **Regulatory definition of ACM: > 1% asbestos.** PLM is the regulatory standard for determining whether a material is ACM.
- **TEM (Transmission Electron Microscopy):** Higher sensitivity and specificity than PLM. Required for air clearance sampling after asbestos abatement (per AHERA). Also used when PLM results are "trace" (< 1%) but TEM confirmation is desired, and for chrysotile identification in materials where serpentine fibers might be confused with PLM.
- **NIOSH 7402 Method:** PCM (Phase Contrast Microscopy) for air fiber counting; counts all fibers > 5 µm long with aspect ratio ≥ 3:1. Does not identify fiber type. Used for occupational monitoring during abatement.
- **EPA AHERA Protocols:** The Asbestos Hazard Emergency Response Act established sampling and analysis protocols for schools; widely applied to commercial buildings as best practice.

### Occupational and Regulatory Limits
- **OSHA PEL: 0.1 f/cc (fibers per cubic centimeter) as an 8-hour TWA.** OSHA STEL: 1.0 f/cc for 30 minutes.
- **ACGIH TLV: 0.1 f/cc (all asbestos fiber types).**
- **EPA NESHAP:** Asbestos National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants regulate demolition and renovation of buildings containing regulated ACM.

### Friable vs. Non-Friable ACM
- **Friable ACM:** Can be crumbled, pulverized, or reduced to powder by hand pressure when dry. Examples: pipe insulation, spray-on fireproofing, acoustic ceiling spray. Friable ACM is high-priority for remediation when it is damaged or deteriorating.
- **Non-Friable ACM:** Cannot be crumbled by hand pressure when dry. Examples: floor tile (vinyl asbestos tile), roofing shingles, asbestos cement board. Non-friable ACM in good condition is generally left in place and managed. Non-friable ACM can become friable when mechanically disturbed (sanded, ground, drilled) — which is why IETI recommends testing before any renovation activities involving suspect materials.

## 4.2 Lead

### XRF Testing
**X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) analysis** is the preferred non-destructive method for lead-based paint assessment. IETI uses an XRF analyzer to measure lead content in paint without sampling.
- **Results reported in mg/cm² (milligrams per square centimeter).**
- **Federal regulatory threshold: ≥ 1.0 mg/cm² = lead-based paint (LBP)** per HUD and EPA definitions.
- XRF provides immediate results and can test hundreds of building components in a day. Testing covers all painted surfaces of concern: interior walls, trim, window sashes, window troughs, doors and doorframes, exterior.

### Dust Wipe Sampling
Dust wipe samples are collected from defined floor areas and horizontal surfaces using wet wipes. The critical application is **post-abatement clearance testing** — confirming lead dust levels are below EPA clearance standards after lead abatement work.

**EPA Post-Abatement Clearance Standards (40 CFR Part 745):**
- **Floors: 40 µg/ft²**
- **Interior windowsills: 250 µg/ft²**
- **Window troughs (channels): 400 µg/ft²**

These clearance values must be achieved before abatement containment is removed and building is re-occupied.

### EPA RRP Rule
The **EPA Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule** (40 CFR Part 745, Subpart E) requires renovation contractors working in pre-1978 homes, child-occupied facilities, and schools to be EPA Lead-Safe Certified, use lead-safe work practices, and provide pre-renovation disclosure to occupants. Contractors must be certified; Wisconsin's DATCP and Tennessee TDEC administer state RRP programs.

### Blood Lead Level Context
The **CDC reference value of 3.5 µg/dL** (updated in 2021, lowered from 5 µg/dL) defines "elevated blood lead level" in children — meaning approximately 2.5% of children tested have levels above this value. There is **no established safe blood lead level for children.** Lead causes irreversible neurodevelopmental damage (reduced IQ, attention deficits, behavioral problems) with no threshold dose below which effects are zero. IETI provides this context to clients with young children as motivation for comprehensive testing, particularly in pre-1978 housing.

## 4.3 Pesticide Residue

### Indoor Pesticide Contamination
Pesticide residues can persist on interior surfaces for decades following professional applications, particularly organochlorine and pyrethroid insecticides. Relevant compounds in residential and commercial settings:

- **Chlorpyrifos (Dursban):** An organophosphate cholinesterase inhibitor used extensively in residential pest control. EPA banned most residential uses in 2001 due to health concerns for children (developmental neurotoxicity). However, chlorpyrifos applied prior to the ban can persist in dust, carpeting, and porous surfaces for 10–20+ years. Properties in agricultural regions of Wisconsin and Tennessee may have additional exposure pathways from adjacent field applications.
- **Permethrin, Cypermethrin, Bifenthrin:** Synthetic pyrethroids widely used for current residential pest control. Lower acute toxicity to humans than organophosphates; however, they are potent neurotoxins to aquatic organisms and some evidence suggests endocrine disruption effects at chronic low-level exposure.

### Analytical Methods
- **ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay) screening:** Rapid, inexpensive, specific to compound class or individual compound. Used for preliminary screening of wipe samples or dust.
- **GC-MS (Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry) confirmation:** Definitive identification and quantitation. Any positive ELISA result is confirmed and quantified by GC-MS. Most certified environmental laboratories offer a comprehensive pesticide panel by GC-MS.

### Sample Collection
Wipe samples from smooth surfaces (windowsills, counters, floors), vacuumed dust from carpeting (1 m² area defined by template), and bulk carpet/dust samples are appropriate media for pesticide residue analysis.

## 4.4 Heavy Metals

### Arsenic
- **Pressure-treated wood (CCA — Chromated Copper Arsenate):** Banned for residential use effective December 31, 2003 (EPA voluntary agreement with industry). Wood treated before this date (decks, playscapes, picnic tables, retaining walls adjacent to gardens) can leach arsenic. EPA action level in soil near CCA structures. Children with frequent hand-to-mouth contact near old CCA structures are primary risk group.
- **Soil testing** around older CCA structures; **wipe samples** from CCA surfaces if young children have contact.

### Mercury
- **Broken CFLs (compact fluorescent lamps):** Each CFL contains 4–5 mg elemental mercury. Breakage requires ventilation and careful cleanup per EPA protocol.
- **Old thermostats:** Pre-2000 mercury tilt-switch thermostats contain a glass ampoule with approximately 3 grams of liquid mercury. Intact, these pose no hazard. Breakage or improper disposal creates contamination risk.
- **Dental office facilities:** Past mercury amalgam preparation areas may have residual floor contamination.
- **Air sampling** for elemental mercury vapor using Jerome 431-X gold film analyzer (capable of detecting down to 0.001 mg/m³). OSHA PEL: 0.1 mg/m³ mercury vapor (8-hr TWA).

### Cadmium
- **Cigarette smoke residue (thirdhand smoke):** Cigarette smoke deposits cadmium on indoor surfaces; concentrations in dust in homes of heavy smokers can be significantly elevated.
- **Some older yellow/orange paints:** Cadmium sulfide used as pigment; relevant in renovation of vintage commercial art spaces.

# SECTION 5: COMMERCIAL INSPECTION SPECIFICS

## 5.1 How Commercial Differs from Residential

Commercial inspection is fundamentally different from residential in scale, complexity, regulatory context, and deliverables:

**Scale and Sampling Requirements:**
Where a residential inspection might include 5–10 air samples, a 50,000 sq ft office building may require 30–60 air samples to adequately characterize each HVAC zone, floor, and area of occupant complaint. Sample count follows the building footprint, number of HVAC systems, number of distinct occupancy areas, and findings from the walkthrough.

**HVAC Complexity:**
Commercial buildings use Air Handling Units (AHUs) serving Variable Air Volume (VAV) boxes distributed throughout each floor. IETI inspects each AHU's supply/return plenum, cooling coils (primary site of biological growth when drain pans overflow or coils are dirty), drain pan condition (standing water = Legionella and Penicillium/Aspergillus reservoir), supply ductwork, VAV box filter insulation condition, and outdoor air damper operation. Economizer cycles and night setback programs can create condensation conditions that promote microbial growth on cooling coil insulation.

**ASHRAE 62.1 Compliance Assessment:**
IETI measures CO₂ levels at representative locations during occupied conditions (including peak occupancy if possible) and calculates whether ventilation rates meet ASHRAE 62.1-2019 requirements. CO₂ measurements are cross-referenced with HVAC design documentation (CFM per zone), occupancy counts, and direct airflow measurements using a balometer/capture hood.

**OSHA General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)):**
In the absence of specific OSHA standards for indoor air quality, OSHA can cite employers under the General Duty Clause if they knowingly expose employees to recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm. Documented poor IAQ (elevated CO₂, mold, elevated VOCs) combined with documented employee health complaints creates potential General Duty Clause exposure. IETI's commercial reports are formatted to assist facility managers in demonstrating due diligence.

**Duration:**
Residential inspection: typically 2–5 hours. Small commercial (< 10,000 sq ft): 4–6 hours. Mid-size commercial (10,000–50,000 sq ft): 6–12 hours. Large commercial: multiple days.

## 5.2 Legionella Risk Assessment

**Background:**
Legionella pneumophila is a gram-negative bacterium that naturally occurs in fresh water environments. In building water systems, Legionella can amplify to levels that cause Legionnaires' disease (pneumonia) or Pontiac fever when aerosolized water is inhaled. Significant nosocomial and community outbreaks have been linked to cooling towers, decorative fountains, whirlpool spas, large HVAC humidifiers, and hospital hot water systems.

**High-Risk Systems:**
- **Cooling towers:** The highest-risk Legionella source in commercial buildings. Warm, aerated water and biofilm-friendly fill media create ideal amplification conditions. Annual cleaning and disinfection, quarterly water testing, and ASHRAE 188 Water Management Programs are required.
- **Large HVAC humidifiers (evaporative or ultrasonic):** Reservoir water can amplify Legionella; aerosolized into supply air.
- **Decorative fountains and water features:** Recirculating water systems with aerosolization (splash/mist).
- **Hospital and healthcare facility water systems:** Particularly hot water systems with dead legs (pipe sections with no flow), tepid water mixing valves, and water heaters set below 140°F.

**ASHRAE Standard 188-2021:**
ASHRAE 188 "Legionellosis: Risk Management for Building Water Systems" establishes requirements for Water Management Programs (WMPs) for at-risk buildings. IETI can assess whether a building's existing WMP meets ASHRAE 188 requirements and identify physical conditions that create Legionella risk.

**Testing Methods:**
- **Culture (ISO 11731):** Water samples cultured on BCYE agar (buffered charcoal yeast extract); results in 7–14 days; gold standard for regulatory compliance and legal documentation. Detection limit approximately 1 CFU/100 mL.
- **qPCR (quantitative PCR):** Rapid (24–48 hours); detects both viable and non-viable Legionella DNA; more sensitive than culture but may overestimate risk (dead cells detected). IETI uses qPCR for rapid screening; culture for confirmation.
- **Action thresholds (as guidance, no US federal regulatory standard):** CDC recommends remediation when culture results exceed 1 CFU/mL (1,000 CFU/L) in potable water systems; cooling tower treatment typically targets non-detect.

## 5.3 Types of Commercial Clients

**Office Buildings:**
Primary concerns: sick building syndrome, ASHRAE 62.1 compliance, HVAC mold, VOC off-gassing from renovation activities or new furnishings, CO₂ accumulation in densely occupied conference rooms. Deliverable: ASHRAE-based ventilation assessment, IAQ characterization report, HVAC inspection findings.

**Schools (K–12):**
Schools are frequently under-ventilated (ASHRAE studies show widespread 62.1 non-compliance in schools) and have deferred maintenance issues resulting in common mold problems. Additional concerns unique to schools: lead paint in pre-1978 buildings (which constitute the majority of the US school building stock); radon in basements (school children spend significant time in classrooms that may be below grade); occupant vulnerability (children ages 5–18 with developing respiratory and neurological systems). IETI can assist with EPA's IAQ Tools for Schools program implementation.

**Healthcare Facilities:**
Immunocompromised patient populations require stricter air quality standards. HVAC must maintain positive pressure in clean areas, negative pressure in isolation rooms. HEPA filtration is required in critical care, oncology, and transplant units. Acceptable Aspergillus concentrations in healthcare settings are orders of magnitude lower than residential — any detectable Aspergillus in HEPA-protected areas is clinically significant. IETI follows ASHRAE 170 "Ventilation of Health Care Facilities" and APIC/CDC healthcare environmental infection control guidelines.

**Multi-Family Residential:**
Common area HVAC systems can transport mold spores between units if not properly maintained. Cross-contamination between units via plumbing chases, electrical penetrations, and HVAC systems is a common source of occupant complaints. IETI performs building-wide assessments including common area air handling units, laundry facilities (high humidity), parking garages (CO, combustion byproducts), and individual units showing elevated complaint frequency.

**Real Estate Due Diligence (Commercial and Residential):**
IETI regularly performs pre-purchase environmental assessments for buyers and sellers. For commercial acquisitions, this fits within the Phase 1/Phase 2 Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) process: Phase 1 (ASTM E1527 — records review, site reconnaissance, interviews, no sampling) identifies "recognized environmental conditions" (RECs); Phase 2 (ASTM E1903 — sampling and analysis) confirms or characterizes RECs identified in Phase 1. IETI's mold, asbestos, lead, and IAQ assessments are typically separate from the ASTM ESA but can be coordinated with the Phase 1/Phase 2 process for comprehensive due diligence.

## 6.2 What's in the Report

Every IETI report contains the following sections:

**1. Executive Summary (Non-Technical):**
A plain-language summary (typically 1–2 paragraphs) stating whether findings are concerning, what the primary issues are (if any), and the most important next steps. Written so a non-technical property manager or homeowner can read this section alone and understand the key takeaways.

**2. Scope of Work and Inspection Conditions:**
Date, time, inspector name and credentials, outdoor temperature and RH during inspection, building description, scope of services performed, limitations of the assessment.

**3. Visual Inspection Findings with Photographs:**
Documented by room/area. Each finding includes: location description, nature of the finding (visible mold, water staining, moisture reading), supporting photograph with location annotation, and a brief interpretation. Moisture readings are reported numerically (e.g., "Moisture content 22% wood equivalent at base of south wall adjacent to window") not just as "elevated."

**4. Laboratory Results with Chain-of-Custody:**
Raw lab data is attached as an appendix. In the body of the report, IETI summarizes results in a clear comparison table: sample location, sample type, key species detected, concentration, and an indication column (Normal / Elevated / Significantly Elevated / Action Required).

**5. Interpretation of Results:**
The professional interpretation section is the core of the report's value. Lab data is compared to outdoor baseline, relevant benchmarks, and prior history if available. The inspector explains why specific findings are or are not concerning — for example: "Indoor Pen/Asp levels of 3,200 spores/m³ compared to outdoor 280 spores/m³ represents an 11× elevation, which is significantly elevated and consistent with an active indoor moisture and mold growth source." This is the section that differentiates a professional interpretation from a raw data dump.

**6. Prioritized Recommendations:**
Recommendations are listed in priority order (Immediate Action / Short-Term / Long-Term) and are specific. Not "address moisture issues" but rather "repair the failed window flashing on the southeast bedroom window, confirmed with moisture readings of 21–24% WME, to eliminate ongoing water intrusion before any mold remediation is undertaken."

**7. Remediation Guidance:**
IETI does not perform remediation. We provide contractor-agnostic remediation guidance based on IICRC S520 standards, EPA guidance, or relevant standard. This may include: recommended remediation scope (IICRC Condition classification), containment requirements, required PPE, HEPA vacuum and air scrubber use, post-remediation clearance testing requirements. IETI will provide a list of qualified remediation contractors if requested, but does not accept referral fees from contractors — this is a critical aspect of our independence.

**8. Inspector Credentials and Laboratory Accreditation:**
IETI inspector credentials are listed, including certification numbers. The laboratory used is identified, along with its AIHA-LAP accreditation number and accreditation scope. Chain-of-custody forms are reproduced in full in the appendix, demonstrating unbroken custody from sample collection through analysis.

## FREQUENTLY ASKED TECHNICAL QUESTIONS — AGENT QUICK REFERENCE

**Q: How is an ERMI score different from air sampling?**
A: Air sampling captures what's airborne at the moment of sampling — a snapshot that can miss settled, non-airborne spores. ERMI analyzes house dust accumulated over months to years, using DNA-based PCR to quantify 36 specific mold species. It's better at detecting Stachybotrys and Chaetomium, which have heavy, sticky spores that don't readily become airborne. Air sampling tells us what you're breathing right now; ERMI tells us what has been growing in your home over time. Both methods are complementary, not interchangeable.

**Q: What is a "normal" mold level?**
A: There is no officially regulated safe level of indoor mold. What we look for is the relationship between indoor and outdoor levels. If your outdoor baseline shows 800 Cladosporium spores/m³ and your indoor levels are 600, that's normal. If indoor levels are 5,000 while outdoor is 800, that indicates an indoor mold source. For certain species — Stachybotrys, Chaetomium — any detection indoors is significant because they essentially require chronic water damage to grow. For Pen/Asp, any meaningful elevation above the outdoor baseline warrants investigation.

**Q: Can you test for mycotoxins directly?**
A: Yes. Mycotoxin testing can be performed on bulk dust samples using ELISA-based screening or LC-MS/MS (liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry) for quantitative confirmation. RealTime Laboratories and EnviroBiomics offer mycotoxin panels for dust samples. We can collect the samples during our inspection and arrange laboratory analysis. However, I want to set appropriate expectations: the presence of mycotoxin-producing species in a building does not guarantee mycotoxins are present, and finding mycotoxins in dust does not directly translate to a health exposure dose calculation. These results are one piece of a broader clinical and environmental picture.

**Q: What's the difference between your inspection and a general home inspection?**
A: A home inspector is trained to identify structural, mechanical, and safety issues — they're looking at roofs, foundations, electrical panels, and HVAC function. They are generally NOT trained to interpret mold sample results, assess indoor air quality against health standards, or perform environmental sampling. Our inspection is specifically focused on environmental health: measuring contaminants, collecting samples, and applying industrial hygiene and environmental medicine standards to interpret what those results mean for your health. The two are complementary. We often work alongside a home inspector on real estate transactions.

**Q: My contractor says the mold remediation is done. Do I really need a post-remediation test?**
A: Yes — and we recommend you use an independent inspector for that clearance test, not the remediation contractor. The entire value of post-remediation testing is the objective, independent verification that the work was done correctly. IETI never does both the pre-remediation assessment and the clearance test on the same job when we have any financial relationship with the remediator — independence is the point. Post-remediation clearance per IICRC S520 includes visual inspection confirming no visible mold remains, moisture measurements confirming sources have been corrected, and air samples from the remediated area compared to outdoor baseline. All three criteria must be met for clearance.

**Q: How should I prepare my home before a mold inspection?**
A: For air sampling accuracy, close all windows and exterior doors at least 4 hours before the inspection (12 hours is ideal if scheduling allows). Run the HVAC system normally — do not turn it off trying to "trap" mold. Do not use air purifiers or whole-house air filtration on a higher setting than normal. Do not disturb suspected mold areas, vacuum, or do heavy cleaning in the 48 hours prior. The goal is representative conditions, not artificially elevated or suppressed concentrations.
