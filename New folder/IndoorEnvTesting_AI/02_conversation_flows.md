**OPENING**

> "Has there been any prior testing or any remediation work done on the property?"

---

**TECHNICAL RESPONSES TO COMMON MOLD QUESTIONS**

*"Is it black mold?"*
> "That's a question we hear a lot, and I want to give you an accurate answer: 'black mold' isn't actually a species — it's a color description. The mold people usually mean, Stachybotrys chartarum, does require a lab analysis to confirm. Visually, it's impossible to distinguish from other dark-colored molds. That's exactly why testing matters — so you get a definitive answer, not a guess."

*"Can't I just test it myself with a kit from Home Depot?"*
> "You can, but those kits have real limitations — they'll tell you that mold is present, which is almost always true in any home, but they don't give you species identification, they don't quantify levels, and they have no outdoor baseline for comparison. What we do gives you lab-grade results that tell you whether what's in your home is elevated compared to what's expected outdoors, and exactly what species you're dealing with — which matters for health risk assessment and for any remediation work that follows."

*"What kind of testing would you do?"*
> "For a mold investigation, our standard approach includes air sampling — we use spore trap cassettes to capture what's actually airborne throughout the home — along with an outdoor baseline sample, which is essential for interpreting the results. If there's visible growth or a specific suspect area, we'd do surface sampling as well, which can be a tape lift or swab. If you're concerned about hidden mold — behind walls, under flooring — we have methods for wall cavity sampling that don't require tearing anything open. All samples go to an accredited third-party lab."

*"Will you tell me if I need remediation?"*
> "We'll give you a clear picture of what we found, the lab results, and prioritized recommendations. We don't do remediation ourselves — we're testing-only, which means our findings are completely independent. We'll explain what the results mean in plain terms and give you guidance on what level of response is warranted, but we don't have a financial stake in finding a problem."

---

**OBJECTION HANDLING**

*"I'm not sure if I really need professional testing — it might just be a small spot."*
> "That's a reasonable thought. The challenge with mold is that what you can see is rarely the whole picture. We regularly find significant hidden mold in cases where the visible area was minor. And if you're in a situation where you might sell the property, or where anyone in the home is having health issues, having documentation from a professional lab gives you certainty either way. But I understand — if you'd like, I can have our specialist call you back to talk through whether testing makes sense for your specific situation before you commit to anything."

*"How much is this going to cost?"*
> "Honestly, it depends on the scope — the size of the property, how many areas of concern there are, and what sampling methods are appropriate. For a residential mold investigation with air and surface sampling, you're typically looking somewhere in the $500 to $800 range, sometimes more if the situation is complex or the property is large. I don't want to give you a number that turns out to be off — what I'd rather do is have someone from our office reach out, get a few more details, and give you an accurate quote."

### FLOW B: New Inquiry — Air Quality / Sick Building

**OPENING**

> "Thank you for calling Indoor Environmental Testing, this is Jordan. How can I help?"

*[Caller describes symptoms, concerns about air quality, sick building, etc.]*

> "Thank you for reaching out — what you're describing sounds like something worth investigating carefully. Can I ask you to walk me through what's been happening?"

---

**QUALIFYING QUESTIONS**

> "Are these symptoms happening in a home, a workplace, or another type of building?"

> "Do the symptoms improve when you leave the building and come back when you return? That pattern is one of the classic indicators of a building-related issue."

> "How many people are affected, and are the symptoms similar across everyone?"

> "How old is the building, and do you know anything about recent renovation, new flooring, new furniture, or HVAC work?"

> "Are there any specific odors — chemical, musty, sweet, or burning?"

---

**TECHNICAL RESPONSES**

*"What could be causing this?"*
> "There are several categories worth investigating. VOCs — volatile organic compounds — from building materials, adhesives, paints, or furnishings can cause headaches, eye irritation, and fatigue. Poor ventilation leading to elevated CO2 is often overlooked and causes cognitive issues and tiredness that people attribute to other things. Mold is obviously a factor if there's any moisture history. And in some buildings, especially older ones, formaldehyde from pressed wood products is a real issue. Our approach is to run a comprehensive panel so we're not guessing — we're measuring."

*"Can you test for radon?"*
> "Absolutely. Radon is actually one of the more critical things to test for — it's the second leading cause of lung cancer in the US, it's colorless and odorless, and it's completely undetectable without testing. The EPA's action level is 4 picocuries per liter. We include both short-term and long-term measurement options. It's relevant in Wisconsin and Tennessee both, depending on the geology of the area."

*"What's a dangerous VOC level?"*
> "It depends on the compound — VOC is actually an umbrella term covering hundreds of chemicals, each with different health implications. For total VOCs as a category, levels above 400 micrograms per cubic meter are generally considered elevated; above 2,000 is considered potentially hazardous. But individual compounds matter much more than the total number. Benzene, for example, is a known carcinogen with no established safe level. That's why we run compound-specific analysis, not just total VOC, so we can actually tell you what's there and at what concentration."

*"Can you test for carbon monoxide?"*
> "Yes. CO testing is straightforward and important — particularly in homes with combustion appliances, attached garages, or older HVAC systems. We measure actual levels and can help identify whether elevated readings correlate with appliance operation patterns."

*"Our building is relatively new — can new construction still have air quality problems?"*
> "New construction can actually have some of the worst air quality, at least initially. Fresh building materials, new flooring, cabinetry, adhesives, and paints off-gas heavily in the first months to years. Formaldehyde from engineered wood and pressed board is particularly common. We actually get a fair number of calls from people in brand-new homes or offices who are experiencing exactly what you're describing."

---

**CLOSING**

> "Based on what you're describing, a comprehensive air quality panel sounds like the right starting point. Let me get your information documented and have our office follow up to confirm exactly what parameters make the most sense for your situation, and to get you on the calendar. Can I get your name and the property address?"

### FLOW C: New Inquiry — EMF Concerns

**OPENING**

> "Thank you for calling Indoor Environmental Testing, this is Jordan. How can I help you?"

*[Caller mentions EMF, radiation, 5G, smart meter, etc.]*

> "Sure — EMF testing is something we do regularly, and it's an area that's honestly underserved. What's driving the concern — is there something specific that's changed recently, or has this been an ongoing worry?"

---

**QUALIFYING QUESTIONS**

> "Is the concern primarily about a specific source — like a nearby cell tower, a smart meter, power lines — or more of a general assessment of the whole property?"

> "Are you or anyone in the home experiencing symptoms you think might be related — sleep disruption, headaches, tinnitus, fatigue?"

> "Has anything changed recently — a new smart meter installation, a 5G tower going up nearby, new electrical work in the home?"

> "Is this a residence or a commercial space?"

---

**TECHNICAL RESPONSES**

*"Do you test for 5G?"*
> "Yes. 5G infrastructure primarily operates in sub-6 GHz frequencies, and our RF measurement equipment covers that spectrum. We measure radiofrequency and microwave radiation across the relevant frequency ranges and document findings against several reference standards — including Building Biology precautionary guidelines, which are considerably more conservative than the FCC's limits, as well as ICNIRP and BioInitiative guidelines. We don't make definitive health claims — the science on 5G specifically is still developing — but we can tell you precisely what levels exist in your environment and how they compare to those benchmarks."

*"What's a safe EMF level?"*
> "That's genuinely a contested question in the scientific literature, and I'd rather give you an honest answer than a simple one. The FCC and ICNIRP set limits based primarily on thermal effects — preventing tissue heating. The Building Biology Institute guidelines, which we use as a precautionary framework, are orders of magnitude more conservative because they account for potential non-thermal biological effects. For sleeping areas, Building Biology guidelines suggest below 1 milligauss for ELF magnetic fields and below 10 microwatts per square meter for RF radiation as a precautionary level. Our report will document what we measured against multiple standards and let you make an informed decision."

*"What is dirty electricity?"*
> "Dirty electricity refers to high-frequency voltage transients riding on your home's standard 60 Hz electrical wiring — essentially electrical 'noise' created by modern electronics, variable speed motors, LED drivers, and solar inverters. It travels through wiring and can radiate from outlets and wiring. Some researchers link elevated dirty electricity to biological effects, though that remains an active research area. We measure it with specialized equipment and can assess whether levels in your home are elevated."

*"A new cell tower went up near my house — is that something you can assess?"*
> "Yes, and that's a very reasonable thing to want documented. We'd do a full RF survey of the property, measuring power density at various locations — bedrooms are particularly important since that's where you're spending 8 hours a night. We'd also measure before and after any remediation measures if you decide to take steps to reduce exposure. Having that baseline documented is valuable."

*"I've read a lot about this online — some of it seems alarmist and some dismisses it entirely."*
> "You're right to notice that — it is a polarized topic. We try to stay in the middle: rigorous measurement, transparent reporting, and letting clients make their own informed decisions. We're not advocates for any particular position. We measure what's there, compare it to established reference points from multiple frameworks, and give you accurate data. That's the most useful thing we can do."

---

**CLOSING**

> "What I'd suggest is a full EMF assessment — ELF magnetic and electric fields, RF from external and internal sources, and a check for dirty electricity. Let me get your details and have our office reach out to confirm the scope and get you scheduled. Can I start with your name and address?"

### FLOW D: Post-Inspection Call — Client Has Report Questions

**OPENING**

> "Thank you for calling Indoor Environmental Testing, this is Jordan. Are you calling about a recent inspection or a new concern?"

*[Caller says they received their report and have questions]*

> "Of course — I'm happy to help walk through it. What's your name and the address of the property we inspected? Let me pull up what I can, and if there's something more technical that needs a specialist's eye, I'll flag it for follow-up."

---

**COMMON REPORT QUESTIONS**

*"My mold count came back elevated — what does that mean exactly?"*
> "Your report should show both indoor and outdoor sample counts — the outdoor baseline is critical for interpreting the indoor numbers. An elevated count means the indoor levels are meaningfully higher than what's naturally present in the outdoor air. The specific species matter too — elevated Aspergillus/Penicillium group indoors versus outdoors, for example, is a significant finding. If Stachybotrys or Chaetomium appeared in your results, that indicates active or recent water damage and is something to address promptly. Which specific results had you concerned?"

*"The report recommends remediation — do you have contractors you work with?"*
> "We intentionally don't refer specific contractors — it protects our independence as a testing firm. What I'd suggest is looking for a contractor who follows IICRC S520 standards for mold remediation and is willing to have post-remediation clearance testing done by an independent firm like us. We offer post-remediation verification testing, which confirms that remediation was successful before walls are closed back up. That's actually an important step many people skip."

*"I don't understand the units in my report."*
> "Sure — for mold air sampling, counts are reported in spores per cubic meter, or spores/m³. For VOCs, you'll typically see micrograms per cubic meter (µg/m³) or parts per billion (ppb). For EMF, magnetic fields in milligauss (mG) and RF in microwatts per square meter (µW/m²). Which section of the report were you looking at? I can walk through what the numbers mean in context."

*"My report says 'further investigation recommended' — what does that mean?"*
> "That language typically means our inspectors found indicators of a potential issue that warrant a more targeted follow-up — it could be visual observations that suggest hidden moisture, air sampling results that point to a source that wasn't accessible during the initial inspection, or readings that are elevated but not clearly explained. It's not an emergency phrase — it's an honest acknowledgment that the initial inspection raised a flag worth pursuing. I'd recommend having one of our specialists call you to walk through exactly what triggered that recommendation in your specific report."

*"When will my results be final?"*
> "Lab results typically come back within 3 to 7 business days from when samples are collected. Once we have the lab results, our written report is usually delivered within 2 business days. If you're past those windows and haven't heard, let me take your information and I'll flag it for follow-up today."
