### MOLD TESTING

**Q: What's the difference between air sampling and surface sampling for mold?**
A: Air sampling captures what's actually floating in the air — the spores you and your family are breathing. We use spore trap cassettes, sometimes called Air-O-Cell cassettes, that pull a measured volume of air through them and are then analyzed by a lab to count and identify spores. Surface sampling is different — it's a tape lift, swab, or bulk sample taken from a specific material or visible growth to identify what species is growing there. Both have distinct value. Air sampling tells you about exposure; surface sampling tells you about the source. For a thorough mold investigation, we often do both.

**Q: What is an ERMI test and when is it useful?**
A: ERMI stands for Environmental Relative Moldiness Index. It's a dust analysis — you collect settled dust from a defined area of the home, and it's analyzed by PCR genetic testing for 36 specific mold species, divided into groups associated with water damage and those that are common outdoors. It's considered more sensitive than air sampling for detecting chronic exposure because settled dust accumulates over time and can reveal mold that air sampling might miss during a single snapshot visit. It's particularly useful when someone has been symptomatic for a long time, when hidden mold is suspected but not located, or for follow-up assessment after remediation.

**Q: Is all black mold dangerous?**
A: "Black mold" is one of the most misunderstood terms in environmental testing. It's a color description, not a species. Stachybotrys chartarum — the mold most people mean when they say "black mold" — is indeed associated with mycotoxin production and requires chronic water intrusion to grow. But it's not the only dark-colored mold, and many dark molds are not Stachybotrys. Conversely, highly concerning molds like Chaetomium or certain Aspergillus species aren't necessarily dark-colored at all. Lab confirmation is the only way to know what you're actually dealing with.

**Q: Can mold be present without visible growth?**
A: Absolutely — and in many cases, the most significant mold problems are invisible. Mold can grow inside wall cavities, under flooring, in attic sheathing, inside HVAC ductwork, and behind built-in cabinetry. Elevated spore counts in air sampling with no visible source is a classic indicator of hidden mold. We have sampling methods, including wall cavity air sampling, that can detect hidden mold without requiring demolition — though sometimes invasive investigation is ultimately needed to locate the source.

**Q: What moisture levels promote mold growth?**
A: Mold needs relative humidity above roughly 60% consistently, or a wet material to establish itself. Different materials have different thresholds — paper-faced drywall, for example, can support mold growth at lower moisture content than concrete. Materials that have been wet — even from a single event — can retain enough moisture to sustain mold growth if not properly dried within 24 to 48 hours. Chronic elevated indoor humidity, even without any specific water event, is often the culprit in ongoing mold problems.

**Q: Do you also do mold remediation?**
A: No — and intentionally so. We are a testing-only company. That independence is important because it means we have no financial incentive to find a problem or to recommend an unnecessary scope of work. Our findings are objective. We recommend working with a remediation contractor who follows IICRC S520 standards, and we strongly recommend post-remediation clearance testing — which we can perform — to verify that the work was successful before walls are closed back up.

**Q: What is post-remediation verification testing?**
A: After mold remediation is complete but before walls are closed, encapsulants are applied, or the space is reoccupied, we come back in and conduct air and/or surface sampling to verify that the spore levels have returned to normal and that no visible or airborne mold remains in the remediation area. This is an important quality-control step that many people skip — and then have no documentation that the work was done properly. We'd rather you have that confirmation.

**Q: Can mold cause health problems?**
A: Mold exposure can cause a range of health effects, though individual sensitivity varies considerably. Common effects include allergic reactions, respiratory irritation, and aggravation of asthma. Some individuals develop hypersensitivity pneumonitis — an immune response to inhaled mold antigens. Certain mold species produce mycotoxins that have more systemic effects. Immunocompromised individuals, children, the elderly, and those with pre-existing respiratory conditions are generally at greater risk. However, I want to be clear — we document exposure, we don't diagnose illness. If you have health concerns, your physician is the right resource; our role is to identify whether there's an environmental source worth investigating.

**Q: What's the difference between mold and mildew?**
A: Mildew is generally a surface fungal growth — it tends to be powdery or flat, grows on the surface of materials, and is generally easier to clean. It's the white or gray growth you see on bathroom grout or on outdoor plants. Mold is a broader category of fungi that penetrates into materials, can grow in hidden locations, and in many cases involves the kinds of species that raise health and structural concerns. The distinction is less important than the testing, honestly — the question worth answering is what's actually growing and at what levels.

**Q: How long does a mold inspection take?**
A: For a standard residential mold inspection, typically two to three hours on-site, depending on the size of the property and the number of concern areas. Larger homes or commercial properties take longer. The inspector conducts a visual walkthrough, moisture measurements, and sets up sampling — then samples go to the lab. Results take three to seven business days from the lab, and the written report follows within two business days after that.

### AIR QUALITY TESTING

**Q: Can you test for radon?**
A: Yes. Radon is actually one of the more important tests we offer — it's the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States and is completely undetectable without measurement. It's an odorless, colorless radioactive gas produced by the natural decay of uranium in soil and rock. The EPA action level is 4 picocuries per liter — above that level, the EPA recommends mitigation. We offer both short-term and long-term measurement options. Radon levels can vary by season and by floor level, so testing protocol matters.

**Q: What VOC levels are dangerous?**
A: It genuinely depends on the compound, which is why a VOC panel that identifies individual chemicals is more useful than a total VOC reading alone. For total VOCs as a category, readings above 400 micrograms per cubic meter are generally considered elevated and warrant investigation; above 2,000 micrograms per cubic meter is considered potentially hazardous by many occupational health guidelines. But for individual compounds, the numbers are very different — benzene is a known human carcinogen with no established safe level, while other compounds have much higher thresholds. Formaldehyde has a WHO guideline of 0.1 ppm for 30-minute average exposure. We always recommend looking at the individual compound breakdown, not just the total.

**Q: What is sick building syndrome?**
A: Sick building syndrome is a condition where occupants experience symptoms — headaches, fatigue, eye and throat irritation, difficulty concentrating — that are linked to time spent in a specific building and improve when they leave. It's distinct from building-related illness, which has a specific, diagnosable cause. Common contributing factors include inadequate ventilation, biological contaminants like mold or bacteria, VOC off-gassing, and high CO2 from poor air exchange. Our protocol for sick building investigations includes a building systems assessment, HVAC evaluation, and multi-parameter air testing to identify the specific contributors.

**Q: What does formaldehyde come from and how serious is it?**
A: Formaldehyde is one of the most prevalent indoor air pollutants. It off-gasses from pressed wood products — particle board, MDF, plywood — as well as from furniture, cabinetry, some flooring products, adhesives, paints, and certain fabrics. New construction and renovations that involve these materials often have elevated levels that decline over time as off-gassing diminishes. It's classified as a known human carcinogen. The WHO guideline is 0.1 ppm for 30-minute average exposure. Notably, levels can be higher in enclosed spaces and on hot days when off-gassing accelerates.

**Q: How do you test for carbon monoxide?**
A: We use calibrated direct-reading instruments that measure real-time CO concentrations. We typically test in areas adjacent to combustion appliances — furnaces, water heaters, fireplaces, gas stoves — and in garages and attached spaces. Importantly, we look for CO levels under different operating conditions, since some sources only produce concerning levels when the appliance is running or under certain pressure conditions. The OSHA permissible exposure limit is 50 ppm over an 8-hour period; acute symptoms begin appearing above 70 ppm; levels above 150-200 ppm are acutely dangerous.

**Q: Can you test for pesticide residue inside a home?**
A: Yes. Pesticide testing is relevant in several situations — after professional pest treatments, in homes near agricultural operations, in older homes that may have been treated with persistent organochlorine pesticides, or when occupants report symptoms. We collect settled dust samples and surface wipe samples for laboratory analysis. Chlorpyrifos, various pyrethroids, and organophosphates are among the compounds we commonly test for. Pesticide residues can persist in indoor environments — especially in carpets and dust — long after the original application.

**Q: Do you test for asbestos?**
A: Yes. Asbestos sampling is particularly relevant in properties built before 1980, when asbestos was widely used in insulation, floor tiles, ceiling tiles, roofing materials, pipe insulation, and textured paints. We collect samples of suspect materials for laboratory analysis. Importantly, intact, undisturbed asbestos-containing materials may not pose an immediate risk — the hazard is when materials are damaged or disturbed and fibers are released into the air. We assess the condition of suspect materials as part of the evaluation.

**Q: Do you test for lead?**
A: Yes. We offer dust wipe sampling and paint chip sampling for lead analysis, which is particularly important in pre-1978 housing. We also offer XRF-based paint testing in some circumstances. Lead dust generated from deteriorating paint or renovation activities is the primary exposure pathway for children, and we take that seriously.

**Q: What are PM2.5 and PM10 and why do they matter?**
A: PM stands for particulate matter. The number refers to particle size in micrometers — PM2.5 is particles 2.5 micrometers or smaller, PM10 is particles 10 micrometers or smaller. Smaller particles are more dangerous because they penetrate deeper into the lungs and can enter the bloodstream. Sources include combustion, dust, mold spores, and outdoor air infiltration. The EPA 24-hour standard for PM2.5 is 35 micrograms per cubic meter. Elevated indoor PM2.5 is associated with cardiovascular and respiratory effects.

**Q: Can you test CO2 levels — why does that matter?**
A: CO2 is a useful proxy for overall ventilation quality. It's exhaled by building occupants, so in a poorly ventilated space, it accumulates along with other pollutants you can't see or smell. Outdoor CO2 is around 400-420 ppm. Indoor levels below 800 ppm are generally considered well-ventilated; 1,000 ppm or above suggests insufficient fresh air exchange; levels above 1,500 ppm have been associated with measurable impairment in decision-making and cognitive performance. It's a relatively simple measurement but a useful diagnostic tool.

### EMF TESTING

**Q: What types of EMF do you measure?**
A: We measure several distinct EMF types because they come from different sources and require different instruments. ELF — extremely low frequency — magnetic and electric fields come from power lines, electrical wiring, and appliances. Radiofrequency and microwave radiation comes from cell towers, Wi-Fi routers, smart meters, cordless phones, and Bluetooth devices. Dirty electricity refers to high-frequency voltage transients riding on standard household wiring. Ground current or stray voltage comes from neutral-to-earth faults in building electrical systems. Each requires specific meters and protocols — a comprehensive assessment covers all of these.

**Q: Do you test for 5G?**
A: Yes. 5G infrastructure primarily operates in sub-6 GHz frequencies, with some millimeter wave deployment in dense urban areas. Our RF measurement equipment covers the relevant frequency ranges. We document findings against multiple reference standards — including ICNIRP limits, FCC limits, and the more conservative Building Biology Institute precautionary guidelines. The science specifically on 5G health effects is still developing, and we don't make health claims — we give you accurate measurement data and let you assess it against whatever standards you find credible.

**Q: What's a safe EMF level?**
A: This is genuinely a contested question, and I think it's important to be honest about that rather than give a simple answer. FCC and ICNIRP limits are set primarily around thermal effects — the level at which tissue heating occurs — and are considerably higher than what the Building Biology Institute considers precautionary. Building Biology guidelines for sleeping areas suggest below 1 milligauss for ELF magnetic fields and below 10 microwatts per square meter for radiofrequency radiation. Some researchers and the BioInitiative Report recommend even lower levels. Our reports present measurements against multiple standards so you can evaluate against whatever framework you consider most appropriate.

**Q: What is dirty electricity?**
A: Dirty electricity refers to high-frequency voltage transients — electrical noise — superimposed on standard 60 Hz household wiring. Modern electronics, variable speed motors, compact fluorescent lights, LED drivers, and solar inverters all create these transients. They travel through wiring and can radiate from outlets and wiring runs. Some researchers have linked elevated dirty electricity to a range of reported biological effects, though the research is not conclusive. We measure it with Stetzerizer or Greenwave meters and report in GS units. Mitigation filters are available if levels are elevated.

**Q: Can EMF testing explain why I'm having trouble sleeping?**
A: Sleep disruption is one of the most commonly reported symptoms among people who suspect EMF sensitivity. Whether EMF is the cause in any specific case is something we genuinely can't determine — we can only measure what's present in your environment and compare it to reference levels. If we find elevated radiofrequency radiation in your bedroom from a nearby cell tower or a smart meter on the other side of a bedroom wall, that's meaningful information. What you do with it is your decision. We can also make recommendations about repositioning sleeping areas or shielding options if levels are elevated.

**Q: What is electromagnetic hypersensitivity?**
A: Electromagnetic hypersensitivity, or EHS, refers to self-reported sensitivity to electromagnetic fields — symptoms including headaches, fatigue, skin tingling, concentration difficulties, and sleep disruption that individuals attribute to EMF exposure. The World Health Organization acknowledges EHS as a reported condition but has concluded that controlled studies have not demonstrated a causal link between EMF exposure and symptoms. That said, many people experience genuine and significant symptoms that affect their quality of life, and environmental measurement is a reasonable step in understanding their environment. We approach EHS clients with respect and thorough measurement — not dismissal.

**Q: A smart meter was just installed on my house — should I be concerned?**
A: Smart meters transmit data via radiofrequency signals, typically in short bursts. Measured power densities at indoor distances from smart meters vary considerably depending on the meter model, installation location, and transmission frequency. Some meters have been measured at levels above Building Biology precautionary guidelines at close range, particularly in sleeping areas or heavily used rooms on the other side of the meter wall. Having a measurement done gives you actual data rather than having to rely on general statements. If levels are elevated, there are shielding options worth discussing.

**Q: Do power lines outside my house cause elevated EMF inside?**
A: Potentially, yes — it depends on proximity, line voltage, and current load. High-voltage transmission lines are a different concern than neighborhood distribution lines. ELF magnetic fields from power lines drop off with distance but can be elevated at significant distances from high-current lines. We can measure the field strength inside and outside your home and document how it varies with distance from the lines. We can also check for wiring errors inside the home that may be creating additional fields unrelated to the power lines.

### PROCESS AND LOGISTICS

**Q: How long does an inspection take?**
A: It depends on the type of assessment and property size. A standard residential mold inspection with air and surface sampling typically runs two to three hours on-site. A comprehensive air quality assessment takes a similar amount of time, sometimes longer if multiple rooms or parameters are involved. A full EMF assessment for a residential property typically takes two to four hours. Combined assessments of multiple types obviously take longer. Commercial properties are assessed individually based on scope.

**Q: When will I get my report?**
A: For anything that requires lab analysis — mold samples, VOC testing, radon — lab results typically come back within three to seven business days from when samples are collected. Once we have the lab results, our written report is delivered within two business days. If you have a time-sensitive deadline, let us know when you schedule and we'll discuss whether rush lab processing is available for your test type.

**Q: Are your labs accredited?**
A: Yes. All samples are analyzed by accredited third-party laboratories — AIHA-accredited for industrial hygiene and microbiology testing, and NVLAP-certified where applicable for specific test types like asbestos. We use independent labs specifically to maintain objectivity — the lab has no relationship with us that would create a conflict of interest.

**Q: Do I need to be home during the inspection?**
A: Yes — we do need access to all areas of the property including basement, attic, and mechanical spaces. We also need to have a conversation with you about areas of concern and your observations of the building. Inspections work much better when the property owner or someone knowledgeable about the building's history is present and available.

**Q: How should I prepare for a mold inspection?**
A: Don't clean or disturb any visible suspect growth before the inspection — we need to see conditions as they are. Don't run air purifiers or HEPA filters for at least 24 hours before air sampling, as these can suppress counts artificially. Keep doors and windows closed for several hours before air sampling if possible. Note any areas of concern you want us to focus on, and gather any information you have about past water damage, leaks, or prior remediation.

**Q: Do you provide remediation services?**
A: No. We are a testing-only company, and that's a deliberate choice. Our findings are completely independent because we have no financial stake in what we find. We'll provide clear recommendations in our report and can explain what remediation standards are appropriate for your situation, but we don't refer specific contractors. Look for a contractor who follows IICRC S520 (for mold) and who will allow independent post-remediation verification testing.

**Q: Can I use your report for a real estate transaction?**
A: Our reports are professional documents with lab results, inspector observations, and recommendations — they are appropriate for use in real estate transactions, including contingency evaluations and disclosure documentation. If you have a specific closing deadline, please communicate that when you call — it affects how we prioritize your inspection and whether rush lab processing is warranted.

**Q: Can your reports be used in legal proceedings?**
A: Our reports are prepared to professional standards and our inspectors have provided expert documentation in regulatory and legal contexts. If you have a specific legal matter, I'd recommend having Martine speak with you directly about what documentation format would be most useful. Please flag this when you call so it can be handled appropriately.

### PRICING

**Q: How much does a mold inspection cost?**
A: A standard residential mold inspection with air sampling — including the necessary outdoor baseline and one to two interior samples — typically runs $500 to $700. If additional samples, surface sampling, ERMI analysis, or wall cavity testing are appropriate, the cost increases. Large properties or complex situations will be higher. Our office provides an accurate quote once they understand the scope of your specific situation.

**Q: How much does an air quality test cost?**
A: A comprehensive residential air quality panel — covering VOCs, formaldehyde, CO2, CO, and particulates — typically runs $700 to $1,200 depending on the number of parameters and the property size. Radon testing, allergen testing, or specific compound testing can be added. Commercial assessments are priced based on scope.

**Q: How much does EMF testing cost?**
A: A full residential EMF assessment covering ELF magnetic and electric fields, RF/microwave radiation, and dirty electricity typically ranges from $600 to $900. Larger properties, properties with complex electrical configurations, or situations involving nearby external sources like cell towers may require more time and measurement points.

**Q: Do you offer any discounts?**

I'd raise that question when the office follows up with you.

**Q: Why is professional testing so much more expensive than a home test kit?**

Professional testing uses calibrated instruments and accredited laboratory analysis, provides species-level identification rather than just "mold present," includes an outdoor baseline for meaningful comparison, comes with a full written report and interpretation, and is done by someone who understands building science well enough to find the actual source rather than just detecting an anomaly. For decisions affecting your health or a major financial transaction, the information quality matters.

### REPORTS AND NEXT STEPS

**Q: What does my report include?**
A: Your report includes a written summary of findings from the visual inspection, all laboratory results with our interpretation, photographs taken during the inspection, comparison of your results to relevant standards and guidelines, and prioritized recommendations. The recommendations are written to be actionable and to give you clear guidance on what level of response is warranted, if any. We try to write reports that are readable for non-specialists while still being technically complete.

**Q: My report says levels are elevated — what do I do now?**
A: It depends on what's elevated and by how much — that context is in the report. For mold: elevated indoor spore counts relative to outdoors generally indicate a moisture source that needs to be found and addressed. For VOCs or chemical findings: the specific compound determines the response. For EMF: elevated levels relative to precautionary guidelines support investigation of the source and potentially exposure reduction measures. I'd recommend reading the recommendations section of your report carefully, and if you have specific questions about what the findings mean for your situation, please call us back and we'll walk through it.

**Q: My report came back clean — is that reliable?**
A: Yes. Our testing protocol is designed to give you accurate results, including accurate negative results. If your air samples show mold spore levels consistent with outdoor baseline, that's a real finding — it means there is no significant indoor mold source affecting the air in the areas we sampled. A clean result is a valuable outcome that gives you confidence in your environment. That said, no testing program covers every possible location or situation, and we document what was and was not assessed in your report.

**Q: Do I need re-testing after remediation?**
A: Strongly recommended, yes. Post-remediation clearance testing — sometimes called post-remediation verification — confirms that remediation was successful and that spore levels have returned to normal before walls are closed back up or the space is reoccupied. This step protects you: it verifies the contractor's work was complete and gives you documentation that the problem was resolved. We offer this as a distinct service.
